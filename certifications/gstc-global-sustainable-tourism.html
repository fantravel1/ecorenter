GSTC: The Global Sustainable Tourism Council

The closest thing to a universal standard for sustainable tourism. Understanding GSTC is essential for evaluating any eco-certification. Here is what it does, what it does not do, and where it falls short.

What Is the GSTC?

The Global Sustainable Tourism Council (GSTC) is an independent, non-profit organization founded in 2007 as a joint initiative of the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) and the United Nations World Tourism Organization (UNWTO). Its mission is to establish and manage global standards for sustainable travel and tourism.

Critically, the GSTC does not certify individual hotels, resorts, or destinations. Instead, it serves as a meta-standard: it defines the criteria that credible sustainable tourism certifications should meet and evaluates certification bodies against those criteria. It is the standard-setter for standard-setters.

The Four Pillars of GSTC Criteria

GSTC has two sets of criteria: one for the tourism industry (hotels, tour operators, attractions) and one for destinations (cities, regions, countries). Both are organized around four pillars:

Pillar A: Sustainable Management

This pillar covers management systems and practices: Does the organization have a sustainability management plan? Is there a designated sustainability coordinator? Are legal requirements being met? Is there transparent reporting? Are staff trained on sustainability? This pillar ensures that sustainability is integrated into operations, not treated as an afterthought or marketing exercise.

Pillar B: Socioeconomic Impacts

Community and economic criteria: Does the operation support local livelihoods? Are local goods and services purchased? Are workers paid fairly? Are there equal employment opportunities? Is the operation accessible to people with disabilities? This pillar addresses the social justice dimension of sustainability that purely environmental certifications often miss.

Pillar C: Cultural Impacts

Cultural heritage and respect: Are local cultural sites protected? Is intellectual property of communities respected? Are cultural artifacts obtained ethically? Do cultural performances involve community consent and fair compensation? This pillar is particularly relevant for eco-tourism in indigenous territories and culturally sensitive areas.

Pillar D: Environmental Impacts

Resource conservation and ecosystem protection: energy efficiency, water conservation, waste management, greenhouse gas emissions, biodiversity protection, light and noise pollution, ecosystem connectivity. This is the pillar most people associate with "eco" certification, but it is only one of four equally weighted dimensions.

Recognition vs. Accreditation

GSTC offers two levels of endorsement for certification bodies, and the distinction matters:

GSTC Recognition

Recognition means that a certification program's criteria have been reviewed and found to align with the GSTC baseline criteria. The review checks what the certification measures. However, it does not verify how the certification body conducts its assessments. A recognized program has good criteria on paper but may or may not implement them rigorously.

GSTC Accreditation

Accreditation is the higher standard. It includes a comprehensive audit of the certification body itself: auditor qualifications and training, assessment methodology, impartiality and conflict-of-interest controls, complaints and appeals processes, surveillance and recertification procedures. Accreditation confirms both what is measured and how it is measured. As of 2026, fewer than 10 certification bodies hold GSTC accreditation.

GSTC-Accredited Certification Bodies

  • EarthCheck: Based in Australia, one of the most widely used certification programs globally with operations in over 70 countries. Strong on benchmarking and data-driven performance measurement.
  • Green Destinations: Focused on destination-level certification. Operates an assessment and awards program for tourism destinations worldwide.
  • Preferred by Nature: Formerly NEPCon, an international non-profit that provides certification and verification services across multiple sustainability standards.
  • Bureau Veritas: A major global testing, inspection, and certification company that has expanded into sustainable tourism certification.
  • Control Union: International certification body offering sustainability certification across agriculture, food, forestry, and tourism sectors.

Strengths of the GSTC System

  • Global baseline: Provides a consistent minimum standard across all countries and cultures, making certifications more comparable.
  • Four-pillar approach: Requires addressing environmental, social, cultural, and management dimensions, not just one area.
  • Independence: GSTC is independent from the tourism industry it serves, reducing conflict of interest.
  • UN backing: Association with UNEP and UNWTO provides institutional credibility and global reach.
  • Continuous improvement: Criteria are periodically updated to reflect evolving understanding of sustainability.
  • Hierarchical system: The recognition/accreditation distinction provides two levels of assurance, allowing travelers to gauge certification rigor.

Limitations and Criticisms

No system is perfect, and GSTC has legitimate limitations that EcoRenter acknowledges:

  • Baseline, not excellence: GSTC criteria represent a minimum standard. A GSTC-aligned certification tells you a resort has met the floor, not that it is exemplary. Many genuinely excellent eco-resorts far exceed GSTC criteria.
  • Indirect verification: GSTC verifies certifiers, not individual businesses. The quality of a GSTC-recognized certification depends entirely on how rigorously the certification body implements its assessments.
  • Cost barrier: GSTC accreditation is expensive, potentially excluding smaller, legitimate certification programs from developing countries where they are most needed.
  • Interpretation flexibility: GSTC criteria are intentionally broad to be globally applicable, but this allows room for interpretation. Two certifiers could both meet GSTC criteria while applying very different levels of rigor.
  • Limited enforcement: Between review periods, there is limited monitoring of whether recognized or accredited bodies maintain their standards.
  • Industry representation: While GSTC is independent, its board and advisory groups include significant industry representation, which some critics argue influences the standard-setting process.

What GSTC Status Means for Travelers

When you see a resort displaying a GSTC-accredited certification (like EarthCheck), it means the resort has been assessed by a body whose procedures have been independently verified. This is a strong positive signal, though it is not a guarantee of excellence.

When you see a GSTC-recognized certification, it means the criteria are appropriate but the implementation has not been independently audited. This is a moderate positive signal.

When you see a certification with no GSTC connection, it could be anything from a rigorous national program to a pay-to-play logo. Additional investigation is warranted.

EcoRenter uses GSTC status as one factor in our evidence tier classification, but we never rely on it alone. Our own assessment of practices, data, and community feedback provides the full picture.

Frequently Asked Questions

The GSTC is an independent non-profit that establishes global baseline standards for sustainable tourism. Founded by UNEP and UNWTO, it does not certify individual hotels but sets criteria that certification bodies must meet, serving as the "certification for certifications."

The four pillars are: (A) Sustainable Management, (B) Socioeconomic Impacts, (C) Cultural Impacts, and (D) Environmental Impacts. All four are equally weighted, meaning a purely environmental certification that ignores social or cultural factors does not meet GSTC standards.

Recognition confirms that a certification's criteria align with GSTC standards (what is measured). Accreditation goes further, auditing the certification body's procedures, auditor competence, and impartiality (how it is measured). Accreditation provides greater assurance.

As of 2026, GSTC-accredited bodies include EarthCheck, Green Destinations, Preferred by Nature, Bureau Veritas, and Control Union. GSTC-recognized programs include Green Globe, Biosphere Responsible Tourism, Travelife, and several national programs.

GSTC sets a minimum baseline, not aspirational standards. It verifies certifiers, not individual businesses. Accreditation is expensive, potentially excluding smaller programs. Criteria are broad, allowing interpretation variability. Enforcement between review periods is limited.
