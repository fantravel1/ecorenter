Certification Profile

EarthCheck: The World's Leading Scientific Benchmarking Program

Founded in 1987 in Australia, EarthCheck is the longest-running and most data-driven sustainability certification for travel and tourism. With over 35 years of benchmarking data across 70+ countries, it sets the gold standard for scientific measurement of energy, water, waste, emissions, and biodiversity performance.

What Is EarthCheck?

EarthCheck is a scientific benchmarking, certification, and advisory group founded in 1987 at the Cooperative Research Centre for Sustainable Tourism in Brisbane, Australia. It is the world's longest-running sustainability certification program for travel and tourism, with more than three decades of continuously collected benchmarking data covering operations in over 70 countries across six continents.

What fundamentally distinguishes EarthCheck from most other sustainability certifications is its unwavering emphasis on quantitative data. Rather than relying on policy reviews, self-reported checklists, or aspirational commitments, EarthCheck measures actual operational performance across five core areas: energy consumption, water usage, waste generation, greenhouse gas emissions, and biodiversity management. These measurements are compared against regional and sector baselines derived from the most extensive sustainability database in the tourism industry.

The program was born from academic research into tourism's environmental footprint, and that scientific DNA remains central to its identity. EarthCheck's benchmarking software was developed in collaboration with environmental scientists and has been continuously refined over nearly four decades to reflect evolving climate science, resource management best practices, and sustainability measurement methodologies.

The Five Core Assessment Areas

EarthCheck's comprehensive assessment framework covers five interconnected pillars of sustainability. Each pillar uses quantitative metrics that enable meaningful comparison across properties, regions, and years.

1. Energy

Energy assessment measures total consumption in kilowatt-hours per guest night, the percentage of energy derived from renewable sources (solar, wind, micro-hydro, biomass), energy efficiency measures including building design, insulation, LED lighting, and smart management systems, and peak demand management strategies. Properties are benchmarked against regional baselines that account for climate zone, so a tropical resort's air conditioning needs are compared against similar tropical properties rather than against temperate hotels.

2. Water

Water benchmarking tracks total consumption in liters per guest night, water recycling and greywater treatment rates, rainwater harvesting capacity and utilization, irrigation efficiency for landscaped grounds, and laundry and cleaning water optimization. In water-stressed regions, EarthCheck applies stricter baselines and gives additional weight to water conservation innovations such as atmospheric water generators and closed-loop treatment systems.

3. Waste

Waste metrics include total waste generated per guest night, diversion rates from landfill through recycling, composting, and upcycling programs, hazardous waste management protocols, single-use plastic elimination progress, and food waste reduction initiatives including composting, donation programs, and guest education. EarthCheck tracks both quantity and quality of waste management, rewarding properties that move beyond recycling to genuine waste prevention.

90

4. Emissions

Emissions tracking covers total greenhouse gas output in kilograms of CO2 equivalent per guest night, scope 1 emissions from on-site combustion and refrigerants, scope 2 emissions from purchased electricity, transportation emissions associated with guest transfers and staff commuting, and carbon offset program quality and verification. EarthCheck requires properties to calculate and report their full carbon footprint, not just the easily measurable direct emissions.

5. Biodiversity

Biodiversity assessment evaluates habitat protection and restoration efforts on and adjacent to property grounds, native species planting programs and invasive species management, wildlife corridor maintenance and connectivity, light pollution reduction to protect nocturnal species, guest education about local ecosystems, and partnerships with conservation organizations. This dimension was significantly strengthened in EarthCheck's 2024 criteria update, reflecting growing recognition that operational efficiency alone is insufficient without ecosystem stewardship.

Certification Levels and Progression

EarthCheck employs a tiered certification pathway that rewards sustained commitment and continuous improvement over time. This progressive structure ensures that certification represents not just a snapshot achievement but an ongoing journey of environmental performance enhancement.

Benchmarking (Entry Level)

At the benchmarking level, properties enter their operational data into EarthCheck's proprietary system and receive a comprehensive performance analysis comparing their operations to regional baselines and peer properties. This level provides the data foundation for certification but does not require an external audit. It serves as an essential diagnostic tool, identifying exactly where a property stands and where improvement is most needed.

Silver Certification

Silver certification requires meeting EarthCheck's performance standards across all five core areas and passing a rigorous third-party audit conducted by an independent EarthCheck-trained assessor. The audit verifies that reported data is accurate, that sustainability management systems are properly implemented, and that the property demonstrates genuine commitment to environmental stewardship. Silver is the entry-level certification and represents a credible, verified baseline of sustainability performance.

Gold Certification

Gold certification recognizes five consecutive years of certified performance with documented, measurable continuous improvement. This level demonstrates not just competence but sustained dedication. Properties must show quantifiable improvement in key indicators over the five-year period, meaning that maintaining the same performance year after year is insufficient. Gold certified properties have proven they are on an upward trajectory of environmental performance.

Platinum Certification

Platinum is the highest level of EarthCheck certification, recognizing ten consecutive years of certified performance with sustained excellence and continuous improvement across all assessed dimensions. Very few properties worldwide hold Platinum certification, making it one of the most prestigious sustainability credentials in the global tourism industry. Platinum properties represent genuine sustainability leadership and serve as models for the industry.

GSTC Accreditation: What It Means

EarthCheck holds full GSTC accreditation, the highest level of endorsement from the Global Sustainable Tourism Council. This accreditation confirms that both EarthCheck's criteria and its auditing procedures have been independently verified against global baseline standards. For travelers and industry professionals, GSTC accreditation provides a critical layer of assurance: it means that EarthCheck certification is not just self-declared rigor but independently validated rigor.

The distinction between GSTC recognition and GSTC accreditation is important. Recognition confirms that a certification body's criteria align with GSTC standards. Accreditation goes further, verifying that the procedures, auditor competence, impartiality controls, and enforcement mechanisms also meet international standards. EarthCheck's accredited status places it in a select group of certification bodies that have undergone this comprehensive external validation.

Strengths of EarthCheck

  • Scientific rigor: Built from academic research with over 35 years of continuously collected data, enabling the most robust benchmarking in the industry.
  • Quantitative measurement: Actual performance data rather than policy review or self-assessment creates accountability that checkbox certifications cannot match.
  • Annual re-certification: Properties must demonstrate performance every year, preventing the "certify once, coast indefinitely" problem that plagues some programs.
  • Continuous improvement requirement: Higher tiers require documented improvement, not just maintenance of standards.
  • GSTC accreditation: Both criteria and procedures independently validated to the highest international standard.
  • Global coverage: Operations across 70+ countries enable genuine cross-regional benchmarking and knowledge sharing.
  • Comprehensive scope: Five interconnected assessment areas ensure that properties cannot excel in one dimension while ignoring others.

Limitations and Honest Criticisms

  • Cost barriers: EarthCheck certification costs range from several thousand to tens of thousands of dollars annually depending on property size and complexity, potentially excluding smaller eco-lodges and community-based operations in developing countries.
  • Operational focus: Historically stronger on measurable operational metrics (energy, water, waste) than on harder-to-quantify dimensions like biodiversity conservation, cultural heritage preservation, and community empowerment.
  • Relative benchmarking: A certified luxury resort consuming 200 liters of water per guest night may score well relative to luxury peers, but its absolute impact exceeds that of an uncertified budget eco-lodge using 40 liters.
  • Corporate bias: More prevalent among larger, corporate-managed properties, creating an unintentional perception that sustainability verification is primarily for well-resourced operations.
  • Snapshot auditing: Annual audits capture point-in-time assessments that may not reflect seasonal variations or temporary lapses in practice.

What EarthCheck Means for Travelers

An EarthCheck-certified property has demonstrated, through independent third-party verification, that its energy, water, waste, emissions, and biodiversity performance meets or exceeds regional baselines for its property type. Gold and Platinum properties have maintained and measurably improved this performance over five and ten years respectively.

When evaluating an EarthCheck claim, travelers should check the specific certification level. Silver provides solid baseline assurance. Gold and Platinum indicate exceptional long-term commitment. Benchmarking status alone, while valuable as a diagnostic tool, does not involve external audit verification and should not be confused with full certification.

EcoRenter's Assessment

EcoRenter considers EarthCheck certification as one of the strongest indicators of genuine sustainability performance in the tourism industry. Its scientific methodology, GSTC accreditation, annual re-certification requirement, and continuous improvement framework set it apart from less rigorous alternatives. EarthCheck Silver contributes to Tier A evidence classification in our system. Gold and Platinum certifications receive additional weighting that reflects the exceptional commitment they represent.
