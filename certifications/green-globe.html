Green Globe: International Certification With 380+ Criteria

One of the longest-running sustainable tourism certifications, Green Globe brings data-driven benchmarking to hotel sustainability. But its membership model raises important questions about rigor and consumer clarity.

What Is Green Globe?

Green Globe is an international certification and benchmarking program for sustainable tourism established in 1993, making it one of the oldest such programs in the world. Based in Los Angeles, California, it operates in over 80 countries and certifies hotels, resorts, convention centers, attractions, transportation operations, and tourism destinations.

Green Globe evaluates businesses against 380+ compliance indicators organized under 44 core criteria. These criteria are aligned with GSTC standards and cover four main areas: sustainable management, social and economic impacts, cultural heritage, and environmental performance.

The Three-Tier System

Green Globe operates with three levels of engagement, and understanding the distinction is critical for travelers:

Member

A business that has joined Green Globe and committed to working toward certification. Members pay annual fees and gain access to Green Globe's tools and benchmarking platform. They can display the Green Globe member logo. However, membership does not mean the business has been audited or verified. It means they have paid a fee and started the process. This distinction is frequently not clear to consumers.

Benchmarked

A member that has submitted operational data (energy, water, waste, social metrics) and received a benchmarking report comparing their performance to industry baselines. Benchmarking provides valuable data but still does not involve on-site verification. The data is self-reported.

Certified

A benchmarked member that has undergone an on-site audit by an independent third-party auditor, verified compliance with Green Globe criteria, and met or exceeded baseline performance levels. This is the only tier that involves actual verification. Green Globe Certified status requires biennial re-auditing and annual benchmarking data submission.

The Benchmarking System: Green Globe's Strongest Feature

Green Globe's benchmarking approach is arguably its most valuable contribution to sustainable tourism. Rather than simply checking whether policies exist (a weakness of many certifications), Green Globe requires members to report quantitative operational data:

  • Energy: Total energy consumption, renewable energy percentage, energy per guest-night, energy per square meter.
  • Water: Total water consumption, water per guest-night, recycled water percentage, rainwater harvesting volume.
  • Waste: Total waste generated, waste diverted from landfill, waste per guest-night, recycling rates by material.
  • Carbon: Total greenhouse gas emissions, emissions per guest-night, scope 1 and scope 2 breakdown.
  • Community: Local employment percentage, training investment, community development spending.

This data is compared against baselines calculated from Green Globe's global database. Members receive reports showing where they stand relative to baseline performance and relative to top performers in their category and region. This creates a powerful improvement incentive: it is one thing to meet a minimum standard; it is another to see that your water consumption is twice the industry average.

Strengths

  • Data-driven: The benchmarking system provides quantitative, comparable metrics that go beyond checkbox compliance.
  • Long track record: Operating since 1993, Green Globe has decades of benchmarking data and institutional knowledge.
  • Global coverage: Certified properties in 80+ countries provide broad geographic reach.
  • GSTC recognition: Criteria alignment with GSTC global baseline standards.
  • Comprehensive criteria: 380+ compliance indicators cover environmental, social, cultural, and management dimensions.
  • Improvement tracking: Year-over-year benchmarking data allows tracking of sustainability improvements over time.

Criticisms and Limitations

Green Globe faces several legitimate criticisms:

The Membership Model Problem

Green Globe's most criticized aspect is the membership tier. Businesses can join, pay a fee, and display the Green Globe member logo before any assessment or audit has taken place. For consumers, the visual distinction between a "member" logo and a "certified" logo is subtle. This creates a potential for misleading consumer perception: a traveler sees Green Globe on a hotel's website and assumes it has been verified, when it may only have paid a membership fee.

EcoRenter considers only Green Globe Certified status (with completed on-site audit) as meaningful evidence. Member and Benchmarked status are not counted in our evidence tier system.

Self-Reported Benchmarking Data

Until the on-site audit stage, all benchmarking data is self-reported. While audits can verify data accuracy retrospectively, the benchmarking reports that members receive before audit are based entirely on what the business chose to report. This creates an incentive to present favorable numbers.

Not GSTC-Accredited

Green Globe is GSTC-recognized but not GSTC-accredited. This means its criteria have been validated but its auditing procedures have not been independently verified by GSTC. Compared to GSTC-accredited bodies like EarthCheck, Green Globe provides less procedural assurance.

Revenue Model Concerns

Green Globe derives revenue from annual membership and certification fees paid by the businesses it evaluates. This is common in certification but creates an inherent tension: the certifier benefits financially from maintaining relationships with businesses, which could influence assessment rigor. GSTC accreditation addresses this through impartiality audits, which Green Globe has not undergone.

What Travelers Should Know

When you see a Green Globe logo on a resort:

  1. Check the tier: Is it Member, Benchmarked, or Certified? Only Certified has been independently audited.
  2. Check the date: Green Globe certification requires biennial re-auditing. An expired certification may indicate lapsed compliance.
  3. Ask for benchmarking data: Green Globe certified properties should be able to share their benchmarking reports showing performance against baselines.
  4. Consider alongside other evidence: Green Globe Certified status is a positive signal but should be one of several factors in evaluating a resort's sustainability.

EcoRenter's Assessment

EcoRenter considers Green Globe Certified (not Member or Benchmarked) as a credible certification that contributes to Tier A evidence classification. The benchmarking approach is particularly valuable for providing quantitative performance data. However, the membership model's potential for consumer confusion and the lack of GSTC accreditation are noted limitations. We recommend travelers specifically verify that a resort holds Certified status and not just membership.
