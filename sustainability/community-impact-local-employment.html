Community Impact in Eco-Tourism: Beyond Green Buildings to Human Dignity

A resort can run on 100% solar power and still exploit its workers. Sustainability without social justice is incomplete. This guide examines the human dimension of sustainable tourism.

The Forgotten Dimension of Sustainability

When travelers think of sustainable tourism, they typically picture solar panels, composting, and wildlife conservation. Rarely do they think about whether the person serving their breakfast earns enough to feed their own family. Yet the social dimension of sustainability, how tourism affects the communities where it operates, is arguably the most important and certainly the most neglected.

Tourism is the primary income source for millions of communities in developing countries. When it works well, it funds schools, builds clinics, and provides dignified livelihoods. When it works badly, it displaces communities, degrades cultural traditions, concentrates wealth among foreign investors, and leaves local people serving drinks in the resort that sits on their ancestral land.

In EcoRenter's database, the average Community impact score is 64 out of 100, the lowest of our five sustainability dimensions. Even resorts that excel in energy, water, and waste management frequently fall short on community impact. This guide explains what genuine community impact looks like and how to distinguish it from community washing.

Local Employment: Numbers and Nuance

The simplest measure of community impact is local employment: what percentage of staff comes from the surrounding community or region? But this single number hides enormous variation in quality.

The Employment Pyramid Problem

In conventional tourism, local employment follows a pyramid pattern. Local community members fill the base: housekeeping, gardening, security, laundry, and kitchen prep. Mid-level positions (front desk, restaurant service, activity guides) may include some locals. Management, finance, marketing, and ownership sit at the top and are almost exclusively held by external investors and expatriates.

This pattern means that while a resort might truthfully claim "80% local employment," 90% of the wage bill flows to the 20% who are not local. The community gets jobs, but not economic power.

What Genuine Local Employment Looks Like

  • Local management: At least some management positions held by community members, with training and advancement pathways for others.
  • Skills development: Formal training programs that build transferable skills: hospitality management, languages, financial literacy, guiding certifications.
  • Living wages: Compensation that covers basic needs (housing, food, healthcare, education for children) in the local cost-of-living context, not just minimum wage.
  • Benefits: Health insurance, paid leave, retirement contributions, and educational support, practices standard in developed countries but rare in tourism in developing nations.
  • Year-round employment: Many tourism jobs are seasonal. Resorts that provide year-round employment through off-season maintenance, training, and community projects deliver far greater economic stability.

Case Study: Il Ngwesi Lodge, Kenya

Community Score: 96. Il Ngwesi is entirely owned and managed by the Maasai community of Il Ngwesi. All staff are community members. Revenue funds a community trust that has built two primary schools, a health clinic, and cattle dip facilities. The lodge's board is elected by the community, and all financial records are publicly available to community members. Guides are trained community rangers who combine traditional ecological knowledge with modern conservation science. This is not a resort that employs locals; it is a community that operates a resort.

Fair Wages: What the Numbers Mean

Minimum wage in many tourism-dependent countries is far below a living wage. In Bali, the minimum wage is approximately $150 per month. In Kenya's rural areas, it is around $80. In Mexico's tourism zones, it is approximately $200. These amounts do not cover basic needs in regions where tourism has driven up the cost of living.

Fair wages in eco-tourism should be benchmarked against living wages, not minimum wages. A living wage covers food, housing, healthcare, education, transportation, and a small amount of savings for a worker and their dependents. Depending on the country and region, a living wage is typically 1.5 to 3 times the legal minimum wage.

Wage Transparency

Very few resorts publish wage data. In EcoRenter's database, only 12% of listed resorts disclose average and minimum wages for local staff. Those that do tend to pay significantly above average, suggesting that transparency itself is correlated with fair practices. We consider wage disclosure a strong positive signal in our Community scoring.

Cultural Preservation vs. Cultural Commodification

Tourism has a complicated relationship with local cultures. At its best, it provides economic incentives to preserve traditional knowledge, arts, and practices that might otherwise be lost. At its worst, it reduces living cultures to commodified performances staged for tourist consumption.

Signs of Genuine Cultural Preservation

  • Cultural activities led by community members who choose what to share and what to keep private
  • Revenue from cultural experiences flowing directly to cultural practitioners and their communities
  • Architecture and design that incorporates and celebrates local building traditions rather than imposing foreign aesthetics
  • Local language used alongside international languages in resort signage and communications
  • Local cuisine prominently featured, prepared by local cooks, using local ingredients and traditional methods
  • Sacred sites and ceremonies respected, with access determined by the community, not the resort

Signs of Cultural Commodification

  • Non-local staff performing "traditional" dances or ceremonies for tourist entertainment
  • "Cultural experiences" that are staged performances bearing little resemblance to actual community life
  • Traditional art sold to tourists with no revenue reaching the artists' communities
  • Sacred symbols used as decorative motifs in resort design without permission or understanding
  • Community village visits that treat local people as exhibits rather than hosts

Indigenous Rights and Free, Prior, and Informed Consent

Many eco-resorts operate on or near indigenous territories. The principle of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC), enshrined in the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, requires that indigenous communities give explicit consent before any development occurs on their lands. This consent must be free (no coercion), prior (before development begins), and informed (with full understanding of the project's scope and impacts).

In practice, FPIC is frequently violated in tourism development. Land is purchased from governments rather than from the indigenous communities that use it. Communities are presented with fait accompli rather than genuine consultation. Consent is obtained from selected individuals rather than through community decision-making processes.

Case Study: Posada Amazonas, Peru

Community Score: 93. Posada Amazonas is a joint venture between Rainforest Expeditions (a private company) and the Ese Eja indigenous community of Infierno. The partnership was established through a formal FPIC process and operates under a 20-year agreement. The community holds 60% equity and majority board representation. All guides and most staff are community members. Revenue has funded a community health post, a secondary school, and a water treatment facility. The agreement includes a full transfer clause: ownership transfers entirely to the community at the end of the contract period. Independent audits confirm revenue distribution matches contractual obligations.

Community Profit Sharing Models

Beyond employment, genuine community impact requires that a meaningful share of tourism revenue flows back to the community. Several models exist:

Community Ownership

The most direct model: the community owns the resort or lodge entirely. Examples include Il Ngwesi (Kenya), Kapawi Ecolodge (Ecuador, now fully community-owned), and several community conservancy lodges in Namibia. Community ownership ensures that all profits benefit the community, though it also places the full business risk on the community.

Joint Ventures

Partnership models where the community holds significant equity (typically 40-60%) alongside a private operator who brings hospitality expertise and marketing reach. This model is increasingly common and can be very effective when the partnership is genuine and the community has meaningful decision-making power.

Revenue Sharing

A fixed percentage of revenue (not profit, which can be manipulated through accounting) flows to a community trust or development fund. Typical rates range from 5-15% of revenue. This is simpler than ownership but gives the community less control and a smaller share of the economic benefit.

Community Development Levies

Per-guest fees (typically $5-25 per night) that fund specific community projects. Common in conservancy-based tourism in East and Southern Africa. These fees are transparent, easy to verify, and directly linked to community benefit, though the total contribution is smaller than ownership or revenue-sharing models.

What Travelers Should Ask

  1. What percentage of your staff is from the local community? What roles do they hold?
  2. Do you pay above the local minimum wage? Are wages benchmarked against living wage calculations?
  3. How does the local community benefit financially from this resort beyond employment?
  4. Are cultural experiences led by community members? Do they control what is shared?
  5. If on or near indigenous territory, was FPIC obtained? Is there a formal agreement?
  6. Do you publish community impact data in a sustainability report?
