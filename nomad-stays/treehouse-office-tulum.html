Treehouse Office Tulum: Elevated Jungle Workspace in the Mayan Riviera

Work from elevated treehouse platforms woven through the Tulum jungle canopy. 120Mbps fiber internet, solar-powered, cenote swimming pool, organic Mayan-fusion restaurant. Monthly stays from $900 in Mexico's most iconic beach town.

The Space

Your desk is in the canopy, your pool is a cenote.

"I have worked in co-working spaces on four continents, and nothing comes close to sitting at a desk 8 meters above the jungle floor with spider monkeys in the neighboring trees. The treehouse platforms are engineering marvels. The WiFi is serious. And swimming in the on-site cenote after a long sprint is the best dopamine reset I have ever found."

— Lucia, Data Scientist, 2-month resident

Set in 3 hectares of protected Yucatan jungle on the inland side of Tulum's hotel zone, the Treehouse Office is a network of elevated wooden platforms built around living ramon and ceiba trees. The structures use steel ring brackets that accommodate tree growth without piercing bark. Walkways connect four main work platforms at 6-8 meters elevation, each with 6-8 dedicated desks, power, and WiFi. The ground level houses an enclosed air-conditioned workspace, the organic restaurant, and a natural cenote swimming pool fed by underground rivers. A 22kW solar array hidden beneath the canopy provides the majority of electricity, and 800 native trees have been planted to restore previously cleared areas of the property.

Canopy Platforms

Four elevated treehouse platforms sit at 6-8 meters above the jungle floor. Each platform hosts 6-8 dedicated desks with ergonomic chairs, monitor arms, and USB-C charging. Ceiling fans provide airflow. Mesh screens keep insects out while maintaining open-air views. The platforms are connected by timber walkways with rope railings and are fully accessible via a central staircase.

Internet Infrastructure

Primary connection: 120Mbps fiber from Telmex, a significant achievement in Tulum's jungle zone. Backup: Starlink satellite with automatic failover at 70Mbps. Directional WiFi 6 antennas beam signal to each platform. Ethernet at all dedicated desks. The network team monitors connectivity daily. Speed tests posted weekly on the community board.

Ground-Level Studio

An enclosed, air-conditioned ground-level studio provides a climate-controlled alternative. 12 desks with full ergonomic setup. Sound-insulated phone booths. A 6-person meeting room with video conferencing. Floor-to-ceiling windows face the cenote pool. This is the preferred workspace during rainy season or peak afternoon heat.

Power & Backup

22kW solar array distributed beneath the jungle canopy generates 70% of electricity. 20kWh battery bank smooths solar intermittency. Diesel generator backup for extended cloudy periods. All platforms and ground studio have surge-protected power. UPS systems protect networking equipment. The solar array's forest-floor placement preserves the canopy aesthetic.

Energy

22kW solar array generates 70% of electricity. Natural ventilation on treehouse platforms eliminates air conditioning for 75% of workspaces. LED lighting powered by independent solar circuits. The ground studio uses high-efficiency mini-split air conditioning. Smart energy monitoring tracks generation and consumption in real time.

Water & Waste

Private well taps the Yucatan's underground river system with UV and carbon filtration. Composting toilets across the property return nutrients to the reforestation project. Greywater treatment garden processes all sink and shower water. Zero single-use plastics. The organic restaurant composts all food waste. 85% overall waste diversion from landfill.

Biodiversity & Reforestation

The 2-hectare reforestation project has planted 800 native trees since 2022, including ramon, ceiba, chicozapote, and mahogany. The treehouse structures are built around 15 living trees using non-invasive bracket systems. Wildlife cameras document spider monkeys, coatis, iguanas, and 120+ bird species on the property. No trees were removed during construction.

Community Contribution

4% of monthly fees support Mayan community education initiatives in nearby Felipe Carrillo Puerto. The restaurant sources 90% from local Mayan milpa farms. All 18 staff members are from surrounding Mayan communities. Weekly Mayan language and cooking workshops for nomads. Monthly volunteer days at the Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve.

Seasonal Guide

Tulum Seasonal Livability Scores

Independent livability assessments for digital nomads by season.

Winter Dry (Nov-Feb): 9/10

Perfect conditions: 25-30C, low humidity, clear skies. Peak nomad season with vibrant community. Treehouse platforms are ideal for all-day work. Cenotes are crystal clear. Tulum beach is stunning. Book early as demand is highest.

Spring (Mar-May): 9/10

Warm (28-33C) with increasing humidity. Excellent beach and cenote conditions. Whale shark season begins nearby. Easter crowds pass through Tulum but the jungle property feels secluded. Spider monkey breeding season brings playful activity to the canopy.

Summer (Jun-Aug): 7/10

Hot (30-35C) with afternoon thunderstorms. Hurricane season begins. The jungle is lush and dramatically green. Ground studio with air conditioning becomes the primary workspace. Cenote swimming is essential. Lowest prices and fewest tourists. Sargassum may affect beaches.

Autumn (Sep-Oct): 7/10

Peak hurricane risk. Heavy afternoon rains. Lush jungle conditions. Lowest prices of the year. The dedicated long-term community bonds closely. Indoor workspace excels. October transitions beautifully back to dry season. Excellent value for committed nomads.
