How Costa Rica Became the World's Ecotourism Pioneer

In the 1980s, Costa Rica had one of the highest deforestation rates on earth. Today, over 25% of its land is protected, forest cover has doubled, and ecotourism is the nation's top revenue source. This is the story of how bold policy, innovative certification, and community action created the world's most successful ecotourism model.

A Country on the Brink

By 1987, Costa Rica's forest cover had fallen to just 21% of the national territory, down from roughly 75% in the 1940s. Cattle ranching, banana plantations, and logging had consumed the country's forests at an alarming rate. The nation, smaller than West Virginia, was losing its biological inheritance faster than almost anywhere else on the planet.

This was not just an environmental crisis. Costa Rica sits at the junction of North and South America, creating one of the most biodiverse corridors on Earth. The country holds roughly 5% of the world's species in just 0.03% of its landmass. Every hectare of forest lost represented an outsized blow to global biodiversity.

But what happened next was extraordinary. Over the following three decades, Costa Rica not only halted deforestation but reversed it. Forest cover climbed from 21% to over 52%. The country created a national park system covering 25% of its territory. And it built an ecotourism economy that generates over $4 billion annually, proving that intact nature is more valuable than extracted nature.

The Foundation: Abolishing the Military

Costa Rica's conservation story begins with a decision that had nothing to do with the environment. In 1948, President Jose Figueres abolished the national military after a civil war. The budget that would have funded soldiers and weapons was redirected to education and healthcare. This single decision created the conditions for everything that followed.

Without a military to fund, Costa Rica invested heavily in public education, producing a literate, informed citizenry. By the 1970s, a generation of educated Costa Ricans began to understand the ecological value of their forests and the economic shortsightedness of destroying them. The environmental movement in Costa Rica was not imported. It grew from within, nurtured by a society that valued education and long-term thinking over military power.

This cultural foundation matters because top-down conservation that lacks popular support rarely succeeds. In Costa Rica, conservation became a source of national identity and pride, not an imposition by distant bureaucrats or foreign NGOs.

Building the National Park System

In 1969, Costa Rica passed its first Forest Law and created the National Park Service. The early work was scrappy and underfunded. Mario Boza and Alvaro Ugalde, the two biologists who built the park system, often used their own money to buy land and hired local farmers as the first park rangers.

By 1990, the country had established 25 national parks, 8 biological reserves, and dozens of wildlife refuges. The system was designed not just to protect individual species but to maintain ecological connectivity, allowing wildlife to move between protected areas through biological corridors.

The parks attracted international attention and, increasingly, international visitors. Monteverde Cloud Forest Reserve, Manuel Antonio National Park, and Corcovado National Park became world-famous destinations, drawing biologists, birdwatchers, and nature enthusiasts from across the globe. The economic potential of ecotourism became impossible to ignore.

Payments for Ecosystem Services: The Game-Changer

In 1996, Costa Rica introduced its most revolutionary policy: Payments for Ecosystem Services (PES, or PSA in Spanish). The concept was elegantly simple. If forests provide services to society, such as carbon sequestration, watershed protection, biodiversity conservation, and scenic beauty, then the people who maintain those forests should be compensated for providing those services.

Funded primarily through a tax on fossil fuels, the PES program pays private landowners between $45 and $163 per hectare per year to maintain forest cover on their land. The program recognized that conservation cannot succeed if it requires landowners to sacrifice their economic wellbeing. Instead, it aligned economic incentives with ecological outcomes.

The results have been remarkable. Since its inception, the PES program has contracted over 1 million hectares of forest. It is widely credited as a primary driver of Costa Rica's forest recovery, demonstrating that paying for nature's services is both feasible and effective.

CST: Certifying Sustainability

In 1997, the Costa Rican Tourism Board (ICT) launched the Certification for Sustainable Tourism (CST), one of the world's first government-backed sustainability certification programs for tourism. CST was designed to differentiate genuine eco-lodges from greenwashing operations that slapped "eco" on their marketing without changing their practices.

The CST system evaluates properties across four dimensions: the physical-biological environment (how the property interacts with surrounding ecosystems), infrastructure and services (energy, water, and waste management), external clients (how guests are educated about sustainability), and the socioeconomic environment (how the property benefits local communities).

Properties receive a rating from one to five leaves, with five representing the highest sustainability performance. The system is audited by independent assessors, and properties must demonstrate continuous improvement to maintain their rating. CST became a model for sustainability certification programs worldwide and demonstrated that government could play a constructive role in driving private sector sustainability.

Community-Driven Conservation

Costa Rica's ecotourism success was never just a government project. Communities across the country embraced conservation when they saw its economic benefits. The Quaker community in Monteverde established the cloud forest reserve that now attracts over 70,000 visitors annually. Indigenous communities in the Osa Peninsula developed community-based tourism that funds both conservation and cultural preservation.

Rural communities that once depended on logging and cattle ranching transitioned to eco-lodge operations, guiding services, and artisanal products for tourists. This economic transition was not painless, but the income from ecotourism consistently outperformed extractive industries, providing a powerful incentive for ongoing conservation.

Women played a particularly important role in community-based ecotourism. Women's cooperatives in rural areas developed handicraft enterprises, farm-to-table dining experiences, and cultural tourism programs that provided income while preserving traditional knowledge and practices.

The Carbon Neutral Pledge

In 2007, Costa Rica announced its intention to become the world's first carbon-neutral country by 2021. While the target date has been adjusted, the ambition drove significant policy action. The country now generates over 98% of its electricity from renewable sources, primarily hydroelectric, geothermal, wind, and solar. The National Decarbonization Plan, launched in 2019, set targets for eliminating fossil fuels from transportation, agriculture, and waste management by 2050.

For ecotourism, the carbon neutral pledge raised the bar. Eco-lodges began investing in on-site renewable energy, electric transportation for guest transfers, and carbon offset programs for international flights. The pledge created a national framework that made sustainability the expectation rather than the exception.

Challenges and Criticisms

Costa Rica's ecotourism story is not without problems. Overtourism threatens popular parks like Manuel Antonio, where visitor numbers have strained infrastructure and disturbed wildlife. Some critics argue that the focus on international tourism has created economic dependency and inflated real estate prices, pricing local communities out of coastal areas.

The CST certification, while pioneering, has been criticized for lacking enforcement and allowing some properties to maintain certification despite declining standards. Agricultural expansion, particularly pineapple monoculture, continues to threaten biodiversity in unprotected areas. And the country's heavy reliance on hydroelectric power faces challenges from climate change-driven rainfall variability.

These challenges do not diminish the achievement. They illustrate that even the most successful conservation models require continuous adaptation and vigilance.

Lessons for the World

Costa Rica's ecotourism revolution offers several transferable lessons. First, conservation requires economic incentives, not just regulations. The PES program proved that paying for ecosystem services works better than simply prohibiting deforestation. Second, certification systems like CST create market differentiation that rewards genuine sustainability. Third, national identity and education are powerful conservation tools. When citizens see conservation as central to their national story, political support follows naturally.

Finally, Costa Rica demonstrates that small countries can have outsized global impact. By proving that a developing nation could reverse environmental destruction while growing its economy, Costa Rica created a model that countries like Bhutan, Rwanda, Slovenia, and Colombia are now adapting to their own contexts.

The journey from 21% forest cover to 52% was not inevitable. It required bold leadership, innovative policy, community buy-in, and decades of persistent effort. But the result speaks for itself: a country where nature is the economy's greatest asset, not its sacrifice.

This article draws on research from Costa Rica's National System of Conservation Areas (SINAC), the Costa Rican Tourism Board (ICT), peer-reviewed studies on Payments for Ecosystem Services, and EcoRenter's direct assessment of over 30 Costa Rican eco-lodges conducted in 2025-2026.

Costa Rica Ecotourism FAQ

Costa Rica's ecotourism transformation began in the 1970s with the creation of its national park system, accelerated in the 1990s with PES and CST programs, and reached maturity in the 2000s when ecotourism became the country's top revenue source, contributing over $4 billion annually.

The Certification for Sustainable Tourism (CST) is a government-backed program created in 1997 that rates tourism businesses from one to five leaves across four sustainability dimensions: environment, infrastructure, client engagement, and socioeconomic impact.

Approximately 25% of Costa Rica is officially protected as national parks, biological reserves, and wildlife refuges. Including private reserves and indigenous territories, the protected area exceeds 30% of the national territory.

Elements are replicable, but the full package required unique conditions: stable democracy, no military budget, extraordinary biodiversity, and investment in education. Countries like Bhutan, Rwanda, and Colombia have adapted specific aspects to their own contexts.
