Sustainability Metrics in Tourism

The numbers that separate genuine sustainability from marketing. Carbon per guest-night, water usage, waste diversion, energy mix, biodiversity indices, and community impact ratios explained with real benchmarks.

Why Metrics Matter More Than Marketing

Any hotel can call itself sustainable. Few can prove it with numbers. Sustainability metrics are the quantitative indicators that transform vague green claims into verifiable performance data. When a resort says it is "eco-friendly," that is marketing. When it reports 8.2 kg CO2e per guest-night, 87% waste diversion, and 94% local employment, that is accountability.

This guide covers the core metrics used to evaluate sustainability in tourism, with benchmarks that help you distinguish industry leaders from average performers. Use these numbers when evaluating properties, asking questions, and holding the industry to measurable standards.

Carbon Footprint Per Guest-Night

What it measures: Total greenhouse gas emissions attributable to one guest staying one night, expressed in kilograms of carbon dioxide equivalent (kg CO2e). Includes energy for lighting, heating, cooling, cooking, laundry, water heating, and water treatment.

Industry benchmarks:

  • Luxury conventional hotel: 30-60 kg CO2e per guest-night
  • Mid-range conventional hotel: 20-35 kg CO2e per guest-night
  • Average eco-resort: 10-20 kg CO2e per guest-night
  • Leading eco-resort: 5-15 kg CO2e per guest-night
  • Off-grid solar property: Under 5 kg CO2e per guest-night

What to ask: "What is your carbon footprint per guest-night, and does it include Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions?" Properties that only report Scope 1 and 2 are omitting the majority of their climate impact.

Scope 1, 2, and 3 Emissions

Scope 1 (Direct): Emissions from fuel burned on-site. Diesel generators, cooking gas, resort vehicles, and heating fuel. Typically 10-25% of total emissions. The most controllable category.

Scope 2 (Indirect - Energy): Emissions from purchased electricity and district heating. Carbon intensity depends on the local grid energy mix. A resort in Costa Rica (99% renewable grid) has near-zero Scope 2. The same resort in Indonesia (60% coal) has significant Scope 2. Typically 15-30% of total emissions.

Scope 3 (Indirect - Value Chain): Everything else: guest flights, food supply chain, construction materials, staff commuting, laundry services, waste disposal, and water supply. Scope 3 typically accounts for 60-80% of total emissions but is rarely measured or reported. Properties reporting Scope 3 demonstrate exceptional transparency.

Water Consumption Per Guest-Night

What it measures: Total freshwater consumed per guest per night, in liters. Includes showers, toilets, sinks, swimming pools, laundry, kitchen, and landscaping irrigation.

Industry benchmarks:

  • Luxury resort with golf course: 1,500-3,000 liters per guest-night
  • Standard hotel: 500-800 liters per guest-night
  • Average eco-resort: 300-500 liters per guest-night
  • Leading eco-resort: 150-300 liters per guest-night
  • Ultra-efficient desert property: Under 150 liters per guest-night

Key conservation strategies: Low-flow showerheads (6-8 liters per minute vs 15-20 for conventional), dual-flush toilets (3/6 liters vs 13 for old models), rainwater harvesting, graywater recycling for irrigation, drought-resistant native landscaping, and linen reuse programs.

What to ask: "What is your water consumption per guest-night, and what percentage comes from rainwater harvesting or recycled graywater?"

Waste Diversion Rate

What it measures: The percentage of total waste diverted from landfill through recycling, composting, reuse, and reduction. Calculated as: (Total waste - Landfill waste) / Total waste x 100.

Industry benchmarks:

  • Average hotel: 20-30% diversion
  • Committed eco-resort: 50-70% diversion
  • Leading eco-resort: 80-95% diversion
  • Zero-waste aspirational: 95%+ diversion

How it is achieved: Comprehensive waste sorting stations, on-site composting of all organic waste, elimination of single-use plastics, bulk purchasing to reduce packaging, repair and reuse programs for furniture and equipment, and partnerships with local recycling cooperatives.

Renewable Energy Percentage

What it measures: The proportion of total energy consumption derived from renewable sources (solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, biomass), expressed as a percentage.

Industry benchmarks:

  • Conventional hotel: 0-10% (grid-dependent)
  • Average eco-resort: 20-50%
  • Leading eco-resort: 70-100%
  • Off-grid property: 100% (by definition)

Important distinctions: On-site generation (rooftop solar, micro-hydro) is more credible than purchasing Renewable Energy Certificates (RECs). Some properties claim "100% renewable" based entirely on REC purchases while consuming grid electricity from fossil fuels. Ask whether renewables are generated on-site, purchased through power purchase agreements, or offset through certificates.

Local Employment Ratio

What it measures: The percentage of total staff hired from surrounding communities, typically defined as within a 50-kilometer radius of the property.

Industry benchmarks:

  • International chain hotel: 40-60% local employment
  • Average eco-resort: 60-80% local employment
  • Leading eco-resort: 80-95% local employment
  • Community-owned property: 95-100% local employment

Beyond headcount: More meaningful indicators include the percentage of management positions held by local staff, whether wages exceed local living-wage benchmarks, investment in staff training, and whether seasonal workers receive off-season support.

Biodiversity Index

What it measures: The diversity and health of biological systems on and around the property, typically using standardized ecological indices.

Common measurement approaches:

  • Species richness: Simple count of species identified on the property, tracked annually through biodiversity audits by qualified ecologists.
  • Shannon Diversity Index: Mathematical formula accounting for both richness and evenness. Values range from 0 (single species) to 4+ (highly diverse tropical ecosystems).
  • Indicator species monitoring: Tracking species whose presence indicates ecosystem health, such as amphibians, raptors, and coral coverage.
  • Habitat connectivity: Whether the property maintains wildlife corridors connecting to surrounding natural areas.

Economic Leakage Rate

What it measures: The percentage of tourism revenue that leaves the local economy through imports, foreign ownership, expatriate wages, and overseas marketing costs.

Industry benchmarks:

  • All-inclusive foreign-owned resort: 60-80% leakage
  • Independent eco-resort: 30-50% leakage
  • Community-owned lodge: 10-20% leakage

Reducing leakage requires local sourcing of food, materials, and services; local ownership or profit-sharing structures; local banking; and investing marketing budgets with local agencies where possible.

Food Sourcing Radius

What it measures: The average distance food travels from source to plate, and the percentage sourced from local producers within 100 km.

  • Conventional resort: 20-40% locally sourced
  • Average eco-resort: 50-70% locally sourced
  • Leading eco-resort with on-site garden: 70-90% locally sourced

Local food sourcing reduces transport emissions, supports regional farmers, provides fresher ingredients, and creates authentic culinary experiences connecting guests to local food culture.

Putting Metrics Into Practice

When evaluating an eco-resort, ask for these specific numbers. Properties that cannot provide them may not be tracking performance, which is itself a red flag. Properties that share data openly, including areas where they fall short, demonstrate the transparency that genuine sustainability requires.

No single metric tells the full story. A property with excellent carbon numbers but poor community employment is not truly sustainable. Sustainability is inherently multi-dimensional, and the best properties perform well across all metrics while being transparent about where they are still improving.
