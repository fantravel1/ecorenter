Mashpi Lodge

The Story

Where the Cloud Forest Meets the Laboratory

Mashpi Lodge sits at 950 meters elevation on the western slope of the Andes, in the transition zone between lowland tropical forest and montane cloud forest. This is the Choco bioregion -- a narrow strip of hyperdiverse forest stretching from Ecuador to Panama that has been identified as one of the world's most threatened ecosystems. Over 90% of the original Choco forest has been cleared for agriculture and logging. Mashpi's 2,500-acre private reserve protects one of the last significant remnants.

The lodge was conceived by Roque Sevilla, former mayor of Quito and a lifelong conservationist who purchased the land specifically to prevent its conversion to palm oil plantation. His vision was radical: build a luxury lodge so compelling that it would generate enough revenue to fund the reserve's protection permanently, while embedding active scientific research into the guest experience. The result is a place where you might eat breakfast watching hummingbirds at eye level, spend the morning pedaling a Sky Bike through the canopy, and end the day examining newly discovered frog species under a microscope with a resident biologist.

Energy: The Mountain Streams Power Everything

Every watt of electricity at Mashpi comes from a micro-hydroelectric turbine fed by the reserve's mountain streams. The cloud forest receives over 3,000mm of rainfall annually -- among the wettest habitats on Earth -- ensuring consistent water flow year-round. The run-of-river system diverts a small fraction of stream flow through the turbine without damming or significantly altering the waterway. Solar panels provide supplemental power during rare low-flow periods. The lodge's contemporary glass-and-steel architecture was designed with passive climate regulation: the building breathes with the forest, using thermal mass and natural ventilation to maintain comfort without air conditioning.

Water: Born from Clouds

In a cloud forest, water is literally in the air. Mashpi's water comes from pristine mountain springs filtered naturally through the forest's root systems. The lodge uses gravity-fed distribution, eliminating pump energy needs. Greywater passes through a constructed wetland system using native cloud forest plants before returning to the ecosystem. The lodge monitors water quality upstream and downstream of its operations to verify zero negative impact on stream ecology. Fog-catching nets supplement water supply during the drier months, harvesting moisture directly from the clouds that give this forest its name.

Waste: Closing the Loop

Mashpi operates a comprehensive waste management system designed for a remote location with no municipal services. Organic waste feeds a large composting operation that produces soil amendment for the lodge's gardens and the reserve's reforestation nursery. Recyclables are sorted, compressed, and transported to Quito for processing. Single-use plastics have been eliminated entirely: water is served in glass, toiletries in refillable ceramic dispensers, and all packaging negotiations with suppliers prioritize plastic-free alternatives. The lodge estimates an 82% waste diversion rate from landfill.

Community: From Loggers to Guardians

Over 80% of Mashpi's staff come from surrounding communities that previously depended on logging and subsistence agriculture. The lodge provides above-market wages, health insurance, and education programs. Former loggers have been retrained as naturalist guides, rangers, and laboratory assistants -- many of them now possessing extraordinary knowledge of the forest they once harvested. The lodge purchases produce from local organic farms, supports a community school, and funds healthcare access for nearby villages. A community reforestation program pays local families to grow native tree seedlings in home nurseries for planting in degraded areas of the reserve.

Biodiversity: The Living Laboratory

The Life Center is not a museum -- it is a working research station. Resident biologists conduct ongoing surveys of amphibians, orchids, butterflies, and birds. The reserve has documented over 400 bird species, 500 butterfly species, and an unknown but growing number of amphibians, including species new to science discovered within the reserve boundaries. Camera trap networks monitor large mammals: pumas, ocelots, spectacled bears, and tayras have all been confirmed as resident species. The butterfly observatory breeds approximately 30 species for release into the wild, supplementing natural populations and enabling close-up observation of life cycles from egg to adult.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

No resort is perfect. Here is what we document alongside the strengths.

Access Road Impact: The 3-hour drive from Quito follows a partially unpaved mountain road through agricultural land. While the lodge provides shared transport, the road itself traverses deforested areas and generates dust and erosion. The lodge has funded road improvements but the broader landscape context remains fragmented.

Price Accessibility: Mashpi is a luxury property with rates starting around $500 per night per person. While the premium directly funds conservation and research, it places the experience beyond most travelers' budgets. The conservation model depends on high-margin, low-volume tourism.

Food Supply Chain: While the lodge maintains gardens and sources from local farms, much of the gourmet menu requires ingredients transported from Quito, adding food-mile emissions. The kitchen balances culinary expectations with local sourcing where possible.

Construction Footprint: The lodge's modern glass-and-steel architecture, while beautifully integrated into the landscape, required significant construction materials to be transported to this remote mountain location. The environmental cost of the initial construction is amortized over decades of conservation funding.

Common Questions

Mashpi Lodge FAQ

Mashpi operates its own micro-hydroelectric turbine powered by mountain streams flowing through the 2,500-acre reserve. The cloud forest receives over 3,000mm of rainfall annually, ensuring consistent flow. The run-of-river design does not dam or significantly alter natural water flow. Solar panels supplement during rare low-flow periods. The system generates all electricity for the lodge including the Life Center laboratories, kitchen, and guest facilities.

The Life Center is a working laboratory where resident biologists conduct ongoing research on cloud forest ecology. Current projects include amphibian population monitoring, orchid taxonomy, butterfly breeding and life cycle studies, and bird population surveys. Guests can participate in night walks with researchers, visit the laboratory, and contribute to citizen science projects. The lodge has published peer-reviewed research in collaboration with USFQ in Quito.

The aerial gondola covers 2 kilometers through the cloud forest canopy at treetop level, offering views of epiphytes, bromeliads, orchids, and bird life invisible from the forest floor. The Sky Bike is a pedal-powered aerial bicycle along a separate cable through the canopy. Both systems run on hydroelectric power and were installed with minimal forest disturbance using existing clearings for support structures.

A controlled breeding facility where guests observe the complete life cycle of approximately 30 cloud forest butterfly species, from egg to caterpillar to chrysalis to adult. Species include the blue morpho and glass-winged butterflies. Bred individuals are released into the reserve to supplement wild populations. The observatory also conducts research on butterfly-plant relationships and pollinator ecology.

The reserve employs dedicated rangers who patrol boundaries, monitor wildlife with camera traps, and prevent illegal logging and hunting. The reserve connects fragmented cloud forest patches, serving as a biological corridor in the endangered Choco bioregion. Revenue from lodge operations directly funds reserve management and research. Camera trap surveys have documented the return of pumas, ocelots, and spectacled bears since the reserve was established.
