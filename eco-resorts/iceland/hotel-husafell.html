The Story

Where Art, Geology, and Sustainability Converge

Husafell has been a gathering place in West Iceland for over a thousand years. The medieval farmstead was mentioned in the sagas, and its geothermal resources have attracted settlers since Norse times. The area sits at the edge of the highland interior, where the Hallmundarhraun lava field -- formed by an eruption in 930 AD, the same year Iceland's parliament was founded -- meets the glacial outwash plains of Langjokull, Iceland's second-largest ice cap.

Hotel Husafell was built to honor this extraordinary intersection of geology, history, and culture. The architecture draws from the lava field landscape: angular forms in dark stone that reference basalt columns, floor-to-ceiling windows that frame glacier views, and interior spaces that flow like lava channels through the building. The hotel has established itself as both a cultural center -- with permanent art installations and a gallery -- and a base for experiencing some of Iceland's most dramatic geology.

Energy: Volcanic Heat, Zero Combustion

Hotel Husafell draws geothermal water from boreholes accessing the same volcanic system that feeds Europe's most powerful hot spring, Deildartunguhver, just 15 kilometers away. This geothermal water heats the entire hotel complex, the canyon baths, and provides domestic hot water. Electricity comes from Iceland's 100% renewable grid, with the nearest generation coming from the Hraunfossar area hydroelectric system. The hotel achieves zero fossil fuel combustion for any energy need. Carbon neutral status is maintained through offset of remaining operational emissions including supply chain transportation.

The Canyon Baths: Art Meets Geothermal

The Husafell Canyon Baths are the hotel's signature feature: geothermally heated pools built into a dramatic basalt canyon, designed by Basalt Architects. The baths use naturally heated water from the local geothermal aquifer, requiring no energy input for heating. The design philosophy was minimal intervention -- the pools integrate with existing rock formations, requiring minimal excavation. Capacity is strictly limited to protect both the experience and the fragile canyon environment. Water is returned to the natural system after use, passing through filtration to remove any contaminants.

Culture: The Artist's Landscape

Husafell has attracted Icelandic artists for generations. The hotel continues this tradition through permanent installations by Icelandic sculptors, a gallery featuring rotating exhibitions by national artists, and an artist residency program that provides accommodation and workspace in exchange for site-specific work. The Husafell sculpture garden sits adjacent to the hotel, featuring works inspired by the surrounding lava field and glacier landscape. Proceeds from gallery sales support the local creative community and fund the residency program.

Glacier Stewardship

Langjokull glacier, visible from the hotel, has lost approximately 18% of its volume since 1890. The hotel partners with glaciologists to educate guests about glacial retreat and climate change impacts on Iceland's ice caps. The Into the Glacier experience -- a man-made tunnel system inside Langjokull -- reveals ice layers hundreds of years old and provides visceral climate education. The hotel donates a portion of excursion proceeds to glaciological research and publishes glacial retreat data on its website annually.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

Glacier Tourism Paradox: Promoting glacier visits raises awareness of climate change but also contributes to tourist emissions that accelerate the very phenomenon guests witness. The hotel addresses this through carbon neutral operations but the fundamental paradox remains.

Vehicle Dependency: Husafell is accessible only by private vehicle. No public transportation serves the area, meaning guests must drive from Reykjavik (approximately 2 hours), adding per-trip emissions.

Seasonal Operations: While the hotel operates year-round, some activities and staffing levels are seasonal, limiting the year-round economic impact on the local community.
