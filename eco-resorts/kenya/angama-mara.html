Angama Mara

Suspended between heaven and earth on the rim of Africa's Great Rift Valley, Angama Mara is a luxury tented camp where 100% solar power meets deep Maasai community partnership. Every guest night funds wildlife research, a women's beadwork cooperative, and education programs that are reshaping conservation across the Mara ecosystem.

The Story

Where the Rift Valley Meets the Sky

The word "angama" means "suspended in mid-air" in Swahili, and no name could be more fitting. Perched at 1,800 meters on the edge of the Great Rift Valley escarpment, the camp looks out over the Mara Triangle -- the western section of the Maasai Mara National Reserve -- from the exact spot where the famous "Out of Africa" picnic scene was filmed. Below, the golden plains stretch to the horizon, dotted with elephants, giraffes, and migrating wildebeest herds numbering in the hundreds of thousands during the Great Migration between July and October.

Founded in 2015 by the late Steve Fitzgerald and his wife Nicky, Angama Mara was conceived as a new model for luxury safari lodging: one where extraordinary guest experience and genuine conservation impact would be inseparable. The Fitzgerald family brought decades of African hospitality experience to the project, but they also brought a conviction that a safari camp's value must be measured not just by thread counts and wine lists, but by its contribution to the landscape and people who sustain it.

Energy: 100% Solar on the Escarpment

Angama Mara operates entirely on solar power. Over 400 photovoltaic panels are distributed across the property, generating electricity for all 30 tented suites arranged in two camps of 15, plus public areas, the kitchen, laundry, and staff village. Lithium iron phosphate battery banks store energy for nighttime use, and the high-altitude equatorial location provides exceptional solar irradiance year-round. The system was designed by specialists in off-grid safari installations, accounting for the escarpment's exposure to wind and cloud cover. Diesel backup generators are maintained for emergency use but operate fewer than 10 days annually. All lighting is LED, hot water comes from solar thermal collectors, and guest rooms use natural ventilation rather than air conditioning, taking advantage of the camp's 1,800-meter elevation where nighttime temperatures drop to a comfortable 12-15 degrees Celsius.

Community: The Angama Foundation

The Angama Foundation is the camp's vehicle for community partnership, and its most visible project is the beadwork cooperative. The Angama Beadwork Studio employs over 30 Maasai women from surrounding communities, producing traditional and contemporary beadwork jewelry and accessories. The studio operates on the camp premises, giving guests direct access to the artisans and their craft. Products are sold in the camp's gallery and through international retail partnerships, with all revenue flowing directly to the women. For many, this represents their first independent income, funding children's school fees and healthcare.

Beyond beadwork, the Foundation funds school construction and teacher training across five primary schools in the Mara region, a scholarship program that has sent students to secondary school and university, a mobile healthcare clinic serving communities with limited medical access, and clean water initiatives including borehole drilling and solar-powered water pumps. Over 80% of the camp's 200-plus staff members are recruited from local Maasai communities, with extensive training programs that have seen community members advance to senior management positions.

Wildlife Research: Protecting the Mara's Predators

Angama Mara is a key partner and funder of the Mara Predator Conservation Programme, one of East Africa's most important large carnivore research initiatives. The program monitors lion prides, cheetah coalitions, and leopard territories across the Mara Triangle using GPS collaring, camera traps, and daily sighting records contributed by safari guides. The data has revealed critical insights into predator movement patterns, human-wildlife conflict hotspots, and the impact of tourism infrastructure on wildlife behavior. Guests can participate in wildlife monitoring game drives and attend evening presentations by research scientists, connecting the safari experience directly to the science of conservation.

The camp also funds anti-poaching patrols in partnership with the Mara Conservancy, which manages the Mara Triangle. These patrols have contributed to a significant reduction in bushmeat poaching over the past five years, with snare removal teams recovering hundreds of wire snares annually from the reserve's borders.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

Flight Access: Most guests arrive by light aircraft from Nairobi's Wilson Airport, a 45-minute flight that adds significant carbon emissions. Road access takes 5-6 hours via Narok, making low-carbon arrival impractical for most visitors.

Luxury Pricing: Nightly rates position Angama Mara among the most expensive camps in East Africa. While the pricing supports genuine conservation and community programs, it restricts access to wealthy international travelers and raises questions about tourism equity.

Water Stress: The Mara region faces increasing water scarcity driven by upstream deforestation in the Mau Forest and climate change. The camp's borehole draws from a shared aquifer, and long-term water security remains a regional concern.

Tourism Pressure: The Maasai Mara is one of the most heavily visited safari destinations in Africa. Even responsible operators contribute to cumulative vehicle pressure on wildlife during peak migration season, and the challenge of managing overall visitor numbers remains unresolved.
