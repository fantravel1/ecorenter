The Story

Born Free's Living Legacy

In 1967, Joy Adamson -- whose book Born Free had captivated the world with the story of raising and releasing the lioness Elsa -- settled at Elsamere on the shores of Lake Naivasha with her husband George. The property became Joy's base for her conservation work until her death in 1980, and it remains infused with her presence: her paintings line the walls, her books fill the shelves, and her spirit of fierce devotion to wildlife protection defines the centre's mission to this day.

After Joy's passing, the Elsamere Conservation Trust was established to preserve the property and continue her work. Rather than becoming a static museum, Elsamere evolved into a living conservation centre -- part guesthouse, part wildlife sanctuary, part education facility. Revenue from guest accommodation funds the centre's environmental education programs, which have introduced over 100,000 Kenyan schoolchildren to conservation principles since the 1980s. The property itself serves as a teaching tool: a demonstration of how human habitation and wildlife can coexist when managed with intention and respect.

Biodiversity: The Colobus Sanctuary

Elsamere's mature Acacia xanthophloea woodland -- the distinctive fever tree forest of East Africa -- provides critical habitat for a resident troop of black-and-white colobus monkeys, one of the most accessible populations in Kenya. The colobus share the canopy with vervet monkeys, while the lake shoreline and surrounding papyrus wetlands support an exceptional diversity of birdlife. Over 350 species have been recorded, including African fish eagles that nest in the property's tallest trees, pink-backed pelicans, malachite kingfishers, and the secretive papyrus gonolek. The centre maintains detailed species lists and contributes sighting data to national databases. Hippos graze on the lakeside lawn at night, and monitor lizards patrol the gardens by day.

Community: Conservation Education

Elsamere's education program is its most significant community contribution. Over 5,000 Kenyan schoolchildren visit annually for day-long conservation workshops covering lake ecology, wildlife behavior, sustainable living, and Joy Adamson's legacy. The workshops are led by trained Kenyan educators and include hands-on activities like water quality testing, bird identification, and tree planting. A research fellowship program supports Kenyan graduate students studying Lake Naivasha ecology, providing accommodation, lab access, and mentorship. The centre also hosts international conservation researchers and maintains a reference library on East African ecology. Staff are drawn entirely from the local community, with training provided in conservation education and hospitality.

Energy: Historic Building Challenges

As a historic property dating from the 1960s, Elsamere faces different sustainability challenges than purpose-built eco-lodges. Solar water heaters provide hot water for guest rooms, and solar panels installed on service buildings generate approximately 40% of electricity needs. The remaining power comes from the national grid. The centre has replaced all lighting with LEDs and installed energy-efficient appliances where possible without compromising the historic character of the buildings. A biogas digester processes kitchen waste into cooking fuel, reducing propane consumption by approximately 30%. The centre's relatively modest energy consumption -- there is no air conditioning, pool, or spa -- means its absolute carbon footprint per guest night is relatively low despite a lower renewable percentage than purpose-built lodges.

Lake Stewardship

Lake Naivasha faces severe environmental pressure from commercial flower farming, which extracts enormous quantities of water and applies pesticides that run off into the lake. Elsamere is an active member of the Lake Naivasha Riparian Association, advocating for sustainable water management and shoreline protection. The property maintains a natural papyrus buffer along its entire lake frontage, which filters runoff and provides fish breeding habitat. The centre monitors water levels and quality, contributing data to long-term lake health studies. Staff participate in regular lakeshore cleanup events, and the education program specifically addresses water conservation, helping children understand the connection between upstream agriculture and lake health.

Gardens and Grounds

The centre's gardens are managed organically, with no synthetic pesticides or fertilizers. Composting provides soil amendments, and rainwater harvesting supplements irrigation during dry periods. Indigenous tree planting continues to expand the fever tree forest, with over 500 native trees planted in the past decade. The grounds are managed to balance guest aesthetics with wildlife habitat -- undergrowth is maintained for bird nesting, dead wood is left for insect habitat, and native groundcover plants replace conventional lawn wherever possible. An organic vegetable garden supplies the kitchen with herbs, greens, and seasonal vegetables, reducing food transport from Nairobi.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

No resort is perfect. Here is what we document alongside the strengths.

Historic Building Constraints: The 1960s-era buildings limit retrofit potential for modern sustainability technologies. Energy efficiency improvements must balance with heritage preservation requirements.

Grid Dependency: 60% of electricity still comes from the national grid, which in Kenya includes a significant fossil fuel component. Further solar expansion is planned but constrained by roof area on historic structures.

External Threats: Lake Naivasha's health is primarily threatened by large-scale flower farming operations over which the centre has advocacy influence but no direct control.

Basic Accommodation: As a conservation centre rather than a luxury lodge, accommodation is comfortable but basic. This limits revenue generation and the budget available for sustainability upgrades.
