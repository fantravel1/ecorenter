Why Kenya

The Birthplace of Community Conservation

Kenya's conservation story is one of the most compelling on Earth. In the 1970s and 1980s, poaching devastated wildlife populations across the country. The response was revolutionary: rather than excluding local communities from conservation areas, Kenya pioneered the community conservancy model -- an approach where local communities own and manage conservation land, sharing directly in tourism revenue. Today, over 160 community conservancies protect more wildlife habitat than all of Kenya's national parks combined.

The eco-resorts listed here operate within this framework. They are not hotels that happen to be near wildlife -- they are integral parts of conservation ecosystems where every guest night generates revenue for anti-poaching patrols, community health clinics, school scholarships, and habitat restoration. The Maasai, Samburu, and other communities are not employees of conservation; they are its owners and leaders.

Common Questions

Kenya Eco-Resorts FAQ

Community conservancies are managed by local communities who own the land and make decisions about its use. Tourism operators lease land from the conservancy, and lease fees plus a percentage of guest revenue go directly to community projects including schools, healthcare, water infrastructure, and livestock programs. Anti-poaching teams are recruited from the community.

Kenya's established conservancies and national parks have robust security infrastructure. The eco-resorts listed on EcoRenter are located in well-managed conservation areas with professional guides, communication systems, and safety protocols. As with any travel, following local guidance and respecting wildlife distances is essential.

Kenya's conservancies protect some of Africa's most iconic species: elephants, lions, leopards, cheetahs, rhinos (both black and white), giraffes, zebras, buffalo, and hundreds of bird species. Some conservancies like Ol Pejeta also protect chimpanzees. The community conservancy model has led to measurable increases in wildlife populations.
