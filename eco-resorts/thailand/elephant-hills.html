The Story

Where Elephants Walk Free in Earth's Oldest Rainforest

Elephant Hills was born from a simple conviction: elephants should never be ridden. While most Thai elephant tourism in the early 2000s still revolved around riding camps and circus-style performances, Elephant Hills chose a different path -- one inspired by African safari lodges where wildlife is observed, not exploited. The result is a luxury tented camp in the heart of Khao Sok National Park that has become a model for ethical elephant tourism across Southeast Asia.

Khao Sok is not any ordinary national park. Its rainforest is 160 million years old -- predating the Amazon by 130 million years. During the last ice age, when glaciers covered much of the northern hemisphere, this forest survived as a refuge for species that went extinct elsewhere. Today, it shelters gibbons whose calls echo across limestone karst towers, hornbills that glide between ancient trees, and the world's largest flower, the parasitic Rafflesia, which can grow over a meter wide.

Elephants: Observation, Not Exploitation

The elephants at Elephant Hills are rescued from logging operations and street begging. They live in a semi-wild environment where they bathe in natural streams, forage freely, and socialize in herds. There are no chains, no bullhooks, no performances. Guests observe from a respectful distance, watching elephant behavior as it would occur naturally. The mahouts -- former logging elephant handlers -- have been retrained as conservation guides who understand elephant psychology and prioritize animal welfare over tourist entertainment. Full veterinary care is funded by tourism revenue, with a resident veterinarian monitoring each elephant's health.

Energy: Working Toward Renewables

Elephant Hills operates a growing solar array supplementing grid power, with plans for full solar transition. The tented camp design inherently reduces energy demands -- open-air construction uses natural ventilation, eliminating air conditioning. LED lighting throughout. The floating camp on Cheow Lan Lake operates entirely off-grid with solar panels and battery storage. Energy consumption per guest is approximately 60% lower than conventional hotels in the region due to the camp's low-impact architecture.

Water: Rainforest Stewardship

The camp sources water from natural springs within the national park, filtered through on-site treatment systems. Greywater is processed through constructed wetland systems before being returned to the environment. Low-flow fixtures in all tents reduce consumption. The floating camp uses a closed-loop water system to prevent any contamination of Cheow Lan Lake. Rainwater collection supplements water needs during the wet season, reducing spring water extraction by an estimated 35%.

Community: 90% Local Employment

Over 90% of Elephant Hills staff come from surrounding communities in Surat Thani province. The resort pays above-market wages, provides comprehensive healthcare, and funds education programs for staff families. Local organic farmers supply produce for the kitchen. Former elephant logging workers have been given new livelihoods as conservation guides. Community workshops train young people in sustainable tourism hospitality. A percentage of revenue funds local school improvements and healthcare access.

Biodiversity: The Ancient Forest Shield

By operating within Khao Sok National Park, Elephant Hills contributes directly to the protection of one of earth's oldest and most biodiverse rainforests. The camp supports wildlife monitoring programs including camera trap surveys for clouded leopards and sun bears. Canoe trips on Cheow Lan Lake double as citizen science expeditions where guests help record bird sightings. The resort funds gibbon rehabilitation in partnership with national park authorities and contributes to anti-poaching patrols in surrounding buffer zones.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

No resort is perfect. Here is what we document alongside the strengths.

Energy Transition Incomplete: While the floating camp is off-grid, the main camp still relies partially on grid electricity from fossil fuel sources. The solar transition is ongoing but not yet complete. Full renewable energy operations are targeted but not yet achieved.

Captive Elephant Debate: Even ethical sanctuaries face philosophical questions about keeping elephants in managed environments. While Elephant Hills elephants have far better welfare than riding camps, they are not fully wild. The resort is transparent about this reality and works toward maximum autonomy for its elephants within safety constraints.

Access Carbon Footprint: Khao Sok is remote, requiring road transfers of 2-3 hours from the nearest airports. Most guests fly to Phuket or Surat Thani, adding significant travel emissions that are not currently offset by the resort.

Waste Management Challenges: Remote location creates waste logistics challenges. While organic waste is composted, recyclable materials must be transported to processing facilities in Surat Thani. Waste diversion rate is improving but lags behind urban eco-resorts.

Common Questions

Elephant Hills FAQ

Elephant Hills has maintained a 100% no-ride, no-performance policy since its founding. Guests observe elephants in a semi-wild environment, watching them bathe, forage, and socialize naturally. There are no chains, no hooks, and no forced interactions. The elephants are rescued from logging and street-begging operations with full veterinary care funded by tourism revenue.

Elephant Hills operates luxury safari-style tented camps. Each tent features a proper bed, en-suite bathroom, hot water, and rainforest views. The Rainforest Camp on Cheow Lan Lake has floating tented accommodation. Both camps blend luxury comfort with minimal environmental footprint through semi-permanent, low-impact construction.

Khao Sok's 160-million-year-old rainforest shelters gibbons, hornbills, langurs, Asian elephants, Malayan sun bears, tapirs, over 300 bird species, and the world's largest flower -- the Rafflesia. Night safaris reveal flying squirrels, civets, and slow lorises. Cheow Lan Lake supports over 100 fish species.

Over 90% of staff are from surrounding Surat Thani communities with above-market wages and healthcare. Local farmers supply organic produce. Former logging elephant handlers have been retrained as conservation guides. Community workshops teach sustainable tourism skills to young people from the region.

Elephant Hills offers complimentary transfers from Phuket (3 hours), Krabi (3 hours), Surat Thani airport (2 hours), and Khao Lak. Overnight buses from Bangkok to Surat Thani take 10 hours. Flights from Bangkok to Surat Thani take 1 hour. The resort handles all transfer logistics upon booking.
