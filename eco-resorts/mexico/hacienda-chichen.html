The Story

Guardians of Maya Heritage

Hacienda Chichen sits on land that has been continuously inhabited for over a millennium. Adjacent to the UNESCO World Heritage site of Chichen Itza, the hacienda was once an archaeological base camp. Today it is one of Mexico's most important community-focused eco-resorts, employing over 90% local Maya staff, preserving traditional knowledge, and protecting a biodiverse garden sanctuary.

The organic gardens are a living pharmacy and pantry. Traditional Maya medicinal plants grow alongside kitchen vegetables and fruit trees. A bird sanctuary within the grounds hosts over 100 species, including rare endemic Yucatan endemics. Community programs support Maya language preservation, traditional arts, and educational scholarships.

Community: Maya-Led Conservation

Over 90% of staff are from local Maya communities. The hacienda funds scholarships, supports traditional medicine practitioners, sponsors cultural festivals, and provides a market for local artisans. The Yucatan Maya Sustainability Foundation, created by the hacienda, channels tourism revenue into education, healthcare, and cultural preservation.

Biodiversity and Gardens

The organic gardens use traditional Maya agricultural techniques alongside modern permaculture. No synthetic chemicals are used. The bird sanctuary provides critical habitat for migratory and resident species. A medicinal garden preserves traditional Maya healing plant knowledge, documented by community elders and shared with visiting researchers.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

Mass Tourism Proximity: Adjacent to Chichen Itza, one of Mexico's most visited sites. Managing environmental impact of proximity to millions of annual visitors is challenging.

Partial Grid Dependence: Solar supplements but does not replace grid electricity. Yucatan grid relies partly on fossil fuels.

Water Table Concerns: Yucatan's limestone karst means all water eventually reaches underground cenotes and the sea. Careful wastewater management is critical.
