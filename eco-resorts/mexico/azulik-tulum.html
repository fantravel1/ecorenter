Azulik

No WiFi. No electricity. No shoes. In a jungle treehouse lit only by beeswax candles, Azulik strips away the digital noise of modern life and returns guests to something older: the sound of waves, the flicker of flame, the cool water of a sacred cenote. Part artist retreat, part radical experiment in disconnection, Azulik asks what happens when you remove everything except nature and attention.

The Story

The Anti-Resort: Art, Darkness, and Attention

Azulik was created by architect and artist Jorge Eduardo Neira Sterkel, known as Roth, who arrived in Tulum in the 1990s when the beach road was still a dirt track and the jungle reached to the shore. His vision was not to build a hotel but to grow one: structures that would emerge from the jungle rather than replace it, made by hand from local materials, without straight lines or right angles, following the organic logic of roots and vines rather than architectural blueprints.

The result is one of the most architecturally distinctive properties in the world. Guest treehouses are woven from bejuco, a tropical vine, by local artisans using techniques adapted from traditional Maya building. Each structure is unique, elevated above the jungle floor on wooden stilts, connected by winding walkways that form a canopy-level village. Curved wooden bathtubs overlook the Caribbean. Mosquito-netted beds open to the sound of the ocean. And at night, when beeswax candles are the only light, the distinction between the human-made and the natural dissolves entirely.

No Electricity, No WiFi: The Philosophy

Guest rooms at Azulik have no electrical outlets, no artificial lighting, no air conditioning, and no WiFi. This is not a cost-saving measure or an infrastructure limitation. It is the core philosophy. Roth's argument is that modern hospitality has become a reproduction of the connected, illuminated, climate-controlled environments people inhabit at home, making travel pointless. By removing these systems, Azulik forces a recalibration of the senses. Guests wake with natural light, sleep with the darkness, feel the temperature of the tropics on their skin, and experience their surroundings without the mediation of screens. The policy is non-negotiable and explained at the point of booking. Common areas and the kitchen use grid electricity for essential functions, but the guest experience is deliberately analog.

The Cenote: Swimming in Sacred Water

The Yucatan Peninsula sits on a massive limestone platform riddled with cenotes -- natural sinkholes where the rock has collapsed to reveal underground rivers of crystal-clear freshwater. For the ancient Maya, cenotes were portals to the underworld, places of spiritual significance and ceremony. Azulik's cenote is accessible to guests through a jungle path, opening into a cave-like pool where sunlight filters through the canopy above and water visibility extends to the limestone floor. Swimming in a cenote is a fundamentally different experience from a pool or ocean: the water is cool, still, and impossibly clear, creating a sense of suspension in time and space that aligns with Azulik's philosophy of sensory reset.

Art and Architecture: Sfer Ik Museum

Azulik hosts Sfer Ik, a museum and gallery space that extends Roth's architectural vision into a dedicated art environment. The museum is a hand-woven structure of monumental scale, with galleries that flow into each other like organic caverns. Rotating exhibitions feature contemporary art, immersive installations, and the work of artists-in-residence who create in response to the jungle environment. The museum is free to visit and serves as Azulik's cultural contribution to the broader Tulum community, offering a space for art and reflection that is open to all, not just resort guests.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

Tulum's Environmental Crisis: Tulum's beach road, where Azulik is located, faces severe environmental challenges: groundwater contamination from inadequate sewage infrastructure, deforestation, cenote pollution, and overtourism. While Azulik's own footprint is relatively low, its presence and pricing contribute to the economic pressures driving Tulum's unsustainable development boom.

Premium Pricing: The no-electricity experience comes with luxury pricing. The aesthetic simplicity of candlelight and breezes costs significantly more than conventional hotel rooms with full amenities, creating an ironic tension between the philosophy of simplicity and the economics of exclusivity.

Water Concerns: The Yucatan's porous limestone means all wastewater eventually reaches the aquifer and cenotes. While Azulik uses a water treatment system, the broader Tulum zone lacks adequate sewage infrastructure, and the cumulative impact of all properties on the aquifer is severe.

Grid Dependency: Common areas and kitchens use grid electricity, which in Mexico's Yucatan region comes partly from fossil fuel sources. The no-electricity policy in rooms is genuine but does not extend to the property's full operations.
