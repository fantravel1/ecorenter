Why Costa Rica

The Country That Invented Modern Ecotourism

In the 1990s, while most countries were still treating nature as an obstacle to development, Costa Rica made a radical bet: it would pay its citizens to protect forests rather than cut them down. The Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES) program transformed a country that was losing forest cover at alarming rates into one that has doubled its forest coverage in just 30 years.

Today, Costa Rica generates 98% of its electricity from renewable sources -- hydro, geothermal, wind, and solar. Over 25% of the country is protected in national parks and biological reserves. The ecotourism industry employs hundreds of thousands of Costa Ricans and has become the country's primary economic engine, proving that conservation and prosperity are not mutually exclusive.

But not every resort flying a green flag in Costa Rica deserves it. EcoRenter verifies each listing through our evidence-based system, separating genuine sustainability leaders from resorts that trade on the country's eco-reputation without earning it.

Common Questions

Costa Rica Eco-Resorts FAQ

Costa Rica protects over 25% of its land in national parks and reserves, generates 98% of its electricity from renewable sources, and has a decades-long commitment to ecotourism policy. The country pioneered the concept of payment for ecosystem services, making it a global model for sustainable tourism development. Its Certification for Sustainable Tourism (CST) program was one of the first government-backed eco-certification systems in the world.

Prices vary widely. Some Costa Rica eco-resorts are luxury experiences comparable to five-star hotels, while others offer budget-friendly options. The key difference is that verified eco-resorts invest in renewable energy, community programs, and conservation, which may be reflected in pricing but also means your money supports genuine sustainability rather than just profit margins.

The Osa Peninsula is Costa Rica's premier wildlife destination, home to 2.5% of the world's biodiversity on just 0.001% of its surface area. National Geographic called it "the most biologically intense place on earth." The Caribbean lowlands around Tortuguero are essential for sea turtle nesting, while the Pacuare River valley offers rainforest wildlife alongside indigenous Cabecar cultural experiences.
