Greystoke Mahale

On the wild shores of Lake Tanganyika, where the Mahale Mountains plunge into Africa's longest lake, Greystoke Mahale is a chimpanzee tracking lodge built entirely from reclaimed dhow wood. Solar powered, deeply embedded in local fishing communities, and partnered with one of the world's longest-running primate research programs. Your stay funds the protection of 800 wild chimpanzees.

The Story

Where Forest Meets the Oldest Lake

Lake Tanganyika is the world's second oldest and second deepest lake, a freshwater sea stretching 670 kilometers along the western edge of the Great Rift Valley. On its eastern shore, the Mahale Mountains rise abruptly from the waterline, their slopes covered in primary rainforest that has never been logged. This forest is home to approximately 800 wild chimpanzees -- one of the largest remaining populations in Africa -- studied continuously since Japanese primatologists first arrived in 1965. It is one of only two places on Earth where you can track wild chimps on foot and return to a comfortable lodge the same evening.

Greystoke Mahale exists because of this convergence: ancient lake, ancient forest, ancient primates. The lodge was designed to be as impermanent as possible in a landscape that demands humility. Built from the skeletons of retired dhow sailing vessels -- the wooden boats that have plied Lake Tanganyika for centuries -- the structures sit directly on the beach with open fronts facing the lake. No trees were felled. No concrete was poured. When the lodge eventually reaches its own end, the materials can return to the earth or be repurposed again, leaving the beach as it was found.

Chimpanzee Conservation

The Mahale chimpanzee community known as M-group has been studied for over six decades, making it one of the most thoroughly documented wild primate populations in existence. Greystoke supports this research through logistical assistance, researcher accommodation, and direct funding. Guest tracking fees contribute to anti-poaching patrols that protect chimps from wire snares set for bushmeat. The lodge employs former poachers as guides and trackers, transforming them from threats into protectors. Health monitoring programs track individual chimps for signs of respiratory disease -- a serious risk from human contact -- and strict protocols limit tracking group sizes and proximity distances.

Reclaimed Materials & Solar Power

Every structural element of Greystoke was built from reclaimed dhow timber, beach-worn and salt-hardened from years on the lake. Local carpenters with traditional boat-building skills shaped the wood into the lodge's six open-fronted bandas, each unique in character. Solar panels provide all electricity, charging batteries that power lights, a small charging station for cameras and devices, and the kitchen refrigeration. There are no generators. The silence is complete: lake waves, forest birds, chimpanzee calls. This is intentional -- sound pollution from generators would disturb both wildlife and the research baseline that depends on natural behavioral patterns.

Fishing Community Partnerships

The villages along Lake Tanganyika's shore depend on fishing for survival. Greystoke employs local fishermen as boat crew, guides, and camp staff, providing steady income that reduces pressure on fish stocks during lean seasons. Fresh lake fish -- including the endemic Tanganyika sardine -- is purchased directly from fishers at fair prices for guest meals. The lodge funds sustainable fishing gear that reduces bycatch of endangered cichlid species. Community revenue-sharing agreements support school construction, teacher salaries, health clinic supplies, and clean water infrastructure in lakeside villages that would otherwise receive no external investment.

Lake Tanganyika Ecology

Lake Tanganyika holds 17% of the world's surface fresh water and supports over 2,000 species, many found nowhere else. The lake's cichlid fish radiation rivals that of Lake Victoria before the Nile perch catastrophe. Greystoke's position on this ancient shoreline carries ecological responsibility: the lodge supports water quality monitoring, contributes to deforestation-reduction efforts that prevent sedimentation from hillside erosion, and advocates for fishing regulations that protect endemic species. Guest education programs explain the lake's extraordinary biodiversity, turning visitors into ambassadors for its conservation long after they leave.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

Remote Access Emissions: Reaching Greystoke requires a charter flight from Arusha or Dar es Salaam, generating significant aviation carbon. The remoteness that protects the ecosystem also necessitates high-emission transport for every guest and supply delivery.

Disease Transmission Risk: Despite strict protocols, human-habituated chimpanzees face respiratory disease risk from visitors. A single flu outbreak could devastate the population. Mandatory health screenings and distance rules mitigate but cannot eliminate this threat.

Limited Capacity: Only 12 guests at a time means high per-person costs and limited revenue for conservation. The lodge cannot scale its impact without increasing pressure on the very ecosystem it protects.

Lake Ecology Pressures: Lake Tanganyika faces broader threats from climate change, sedimentation from deforestation upstream, and overfishing by industrial vessels that the lodge has limited ability to address.

Supply Chain Carbon: All food, equipment, and maintenance supplies must arrive by boat or aircraft. There is no low-carbon supply option for this location, and the lodge's operational footprint is elevated by the logistics of extreme remoteness.

Common Questions

Greystoke Mahale FAQ

Access is by light aircraft from Arusha or Dar es Salaam to Mahale airstrip, followed by a dhow boat ride along Lake Tanganyika's shore. There are no roads. The lodge accommodates only 12 guests at a time. The journey takes most of a day but sailing past fishing villages and forested mountains is itself a remarkable experience.

Mahale protects approximately 800 chimps with one habituated community of 60 individuals. Tracking starts early morning with local guides who know individual chimps by name. You spend one hour observing at close range -- grooming, playing, feeding, and social interactions. Success rates exceed 90%. Maximum 6 visitors per tracking group per day.

Timber salvaged from decommissioned dhow sailing vessels on Lake Tanganyika was repurposed by local carpenters into six open-fronted bandas. No standing trees were felled. The aesthetic is raw, maritime, and deeply connected to the lake's fishing culture. Subsequent repairs continue using reclaimed materials.

Local fishermen are employed as boat operators, guides, and staff. Fresh fish is purchased at fair prices for guest meals. Sustainable fishing gear has been funded to reduce bycatch. Revenue-sharing funds schools, health clinics, and clean water projects in lakeside villages that would otherwise receive no external investment.

Greystoke collaborates with the Mahale Mountains Chimpanzee Research Project, running continuously since the 1960s. The lodge provides logistical support, researcher accommodation, and funding for anti-poaching patrols and health monitoring programs. Six decades of data make Mahale one of the most valuable primate research sites on Earth.
