Chole Mjini Lodge

Seven open-sided treehouses perch in ancient baobabs on the ruins of a 12th-century Swahili trading settlement. Community-owned, powered by solar panels and candlelight, this lodge on Chole Island near Mafia funds marine conservation, craft training, and the island's school and health clinic. No electricity grid. No walls. Just ocean, forest, and a community that owns its own future.

The Story

Ruins, Roots, and Reef

Chole Island sits in the Mafia Archipelago, 160 kilometers south of Dar es Salaam in the Indian Ocean. For centuries, it was a prosperous Swahili trading hub, its stone buildings hosting merchants from Arabia, Persia, India, and the East African coast. When trade routes shifted, Chole was abandoned. The forest reclaimed the ruins. Baobab roots wrapped around coral-stone walls. Fig trees grew through roofless mosques. Today, a small fishing community of around 1,000 people lives alongside these archaeological remnants, and seven treehouses are suspended in the forest canopy above them.

Chole Mjini Lodge was founded with a radical premise: that the island community would own and govern the tourism that happens on their island. The Chole Society Fund, controlled by an elected committee of islanders, receives a direct share of all guest revenue. This fund has built the island's first primary school, staffed a health dispensary, drilled clean water boreholes, and funded craft training programs that provide income independent of fishing or tourism employment. The lodge is the economic engine, but the community holds the steering wheel.

Marine Conservation

The Mafia Island Marine Park protects one of the richest coral reef ecosystems in the western Indian Ocean, including over 400 fish species and critical turtle nesting beaches. Chole Mjini funds community marine patrols that combat illegal dynamite fishing and drag-netting -- practices that have devastated reefs across coastal Tanzania. A coral monitoring program trains local divers to survey reef health, creating a community-owned dataset on coral cover, bleaching, and fish populations. The lodge sponsors sea turtle nest protection on nearby beaches. Guest snorkeling and diving fees flow directly to marine park management. Mangrove reforestation on Chole's coastline restores critical fish nursery habitat that supports both marine ecology and community fishing livelihoods.

Off-Grid by Design

Chole Island has never been connected to the national electricity grid, and the lodge embraces this reality rather than fighting it. A small solar array charges batteries for LED common-area lighting and a guest device charging station. Beyond that, the lodge runs on candlelight and kerosene lanterns. Treehouses have no electrical outlets. Cooking uses sustainably sourced charcoal. This is not deprivation -- it is design. Guests rediscover natural light cycles, eat by candlelight over the ocean, and sleep in a darkness so complete that the Milky Way paints the sky directly overhead. The energy footprint per guest is among the lowest of any accommodation in this database.

Craft Training & Cultural Preservation

The Chole Society Fund finances craft workshops where islanders, particularly women and young people, learn traditional Swahili woodcarving, coconut fiber rope-making, makuti palm weaving, and boat-building. A sewing workshop produces goods sold in the lodge shop and mainland markets. Over 100 islanders have been trained, creating income streams beyond fishing. Guests visit workshops, learn techniques, and buy directly from artisans. The program preserves cultural skills that were disappearing as younger generations sought mainland employment, anchoring traditional knowledge on the island for future generations.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

Basic Amenities: The deliberately rustic approach means no hot running water, no electricity in rooms, and no air conditioning. This is not for every traveler, and the lodge is transparent about the experience level.

Charcoal Dependence: While sourced from managed forestry, charcoal cooking still contributes to deforestation pressures in mainland Tanzania. Transitioning to solar cooking would improve the energy profile.

Marine Park Enforcement: Despite patrol support, illegal fishing continues in the marine park. The lodge's conservation impact is limited by broader enforcement challenges and mainland demand for reef fish.

Access Logistics: Reaching Mafia Island requires flights from Dar es Salaam or a long ferry journey, adding transport emissions. The remote location limits guest volumes but also constrains conservation funding.
