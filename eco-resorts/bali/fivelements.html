The Story

Five Elements, One Purpose: Healing Through Harmony

The Ayung River is sacred in Balinese Hindu cosmology -- a purifying force that connects the mountains to the sea, the divine to the earthly. Fivelements was built along its banks with a singular vision: create a space where human wellness and environmental wellness are inseparable. Founded by Lahra and Chicco Tatriele after decades of studying Balinese healing traditions, the retreat opened in 2010 and quickly became one of the most awarded eco-wellness destinations in Southeast Asia.

The name references the Balinese Panca Mahabhuta -- five elements (earth, water, fire, air, ether) that must exist in balance for health and harmony. Every design decision, every menu choice, every healing program at Fivelements is informed by this philosophy. The bamboo structures breathe with the air element. The river embodies water. The volcanic soil grounds earth. Fire ceremonies mark transitions. And ether -- the space between -- is honored in the silence of meditation gardens and the pauses between treatments.

Architecture: Bamboo That Breathes

Fivelements' structures are masterworks of bamboo architecture, designed to follow the natural contours of the river valley. The Sakti Dining Room is a soaring open-air pavilion that seems to float above the river, its bamboo columns reaching upward like forest canopy. Treatment rooms are nestled into the hillside, their curved bamboo walls creating cocoon-like spaces that amplify the sounds of flowing water. The design maximizes natural ventilation -- the river valley creates a constant gentle breeze that eliminates the need for air conditioning. Natural light floods every space through carefully positioned openings, reducing daytime electricity use to near zero. All bamboo is locally sourced from managed groves and treated with borax solutions that prevent insect damage without toxic chemicals.

Cuisine: Plant-Based as Philosophy

Fivelements serves exclusively plant-based cuisine, and this is not a marketing decision -- it is a philosophical commitment. The Sakti Dining Room prepares raw, organic, and living food that has won international culinary recognition. Ingredients are sourced from the retreat's own gardens and from a network of certified organic farms within Bali. The plant-based menu reduces the food carbon footprint by an estimated 60% compared to conventional resort dining. But the commitment goes deeper: food preparation follows Balinese principles of energy, with specific ingredients selected for their healing properties. The kitchen operates on a zero-waste model, composting all organic matter and using vegetable trimmings in broths and fermentations.

Wellness: Ancient and Evidence-Based

The healing programs at Fivelements integrate Balinese traditional medicine -- which has been practiced for over a millennium -- with modern wellness science. Balinese healers (Balian) offer consultations alongside yoga instructors, meditation teachers, and nutritionists. Traditional Balinese water purification ceremonies (Melukat) are conducted at the river. Herbal medicine workshops use plants from the property's medicinal garden. Every treatment protocol has been documented and refined over the retreat's history, creating a body of practice that honors tradition while meeting contemporary expectations for evidence and safety.

Community and Water Stewardship

Fivelements employs approximately 90% of its staff from surrounding villages, with particular emphasis on preserving traditional Balinese healing arts by providing a livelihood for practitioners. The retreat has invested in water purification infrastructure that benefits the local community beyond its own operations. Greywater treatment systems return clean water to the watershed. The retreat participates in Ayung River conservation efforts and has funded river cleanup campaigns that have removed tons of plastic waste from upstream sources.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

Partial Grid Dependence: While bamboo architecture reduces energy needs dramatically, the retreat remains partially dependent on Bali's fossil-fuel-powered electrical grid for lighting and equipment. Solar panel installation is planned but not yet complete.

Exclusive Pricing: Healing retreats at Fivelements are premium-priced, placing them among the most expensive wellness experiences in Bali. This limits access to a narrow demographic.

Wellness Tourism Concerns: The broader wellness tourism industry in Bali has been criticized for cultural appropriation of traditional practices. While Fivelements works directly with Balinese healers and compensates fairly, the industry context raises valid questions.

River Vulnerability: The Ayung River faces pollution pressures from upstream development and agriculture. Despite Fivelements' conservation efforts, the river ecosystem continues to degrade due to factors beyond the retreat's control.
