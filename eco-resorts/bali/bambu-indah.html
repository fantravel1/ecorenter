Bambu Indah

The Story

Beautiful Bamboo, Ancient Houses, Living Systems

When John and Cynthia Hardy began collecting antique Javanese bridal houses -- joglos destined for demolition as Java modernized -- they did not intend to build a hotel. They intended to save cultural heritage. But as the collection of century-old teak structures grew on their hillside property above the Ayung River gorge near Ubud, visitors kept asking to stay. Bambu Indah was born from that convergence of heritage preservation and hospitality, and it has become one of the world's most influential demonstrations of what sustainable accommodation can look like.

Each joglo has been painstakingly restored by Balinese and Javanese master carpenters using traditional techniques, then adapted with modern bamboo additions designed by Ibuku, the Hardy family's bamboo architecture firm. The result is structures that are simultaneously museum pieces and luxury accommodations -- places where you sleep surrounded by 100-year-old carved teak panels and wake to the sounds of the Ayung River valley below.

Architecture: Bamboo as Revolution

Bambu Indah is a showcase for what bamboo can do. The material grows to harvestable size in 3-5 years (versus 30-80 years for hardwood timber), sequesters carbon as it grows, requires no pesticides or fertilizers, and can be harvested without killing the plant. The Hardys, through their Ibuku firm and Green School, have developed proprietary bamboo treatment methods that extend structural lifespan to 25+ years. At Bambu Indah, bamboo forms bridges, bathrooms, restaurant structures, and the iconic open-air pavilions that overlook the river valley. Every bamboo pole is locally sourced from managed groves on Bali and neighboring islands.

Food: 60% From the Garden

The permaculture gardens at Bambu Indah are not a decorative afterthought -- they are a functioning food production system that supplies approximately 60% of the kitchen's fresh produce. Tropical fruits, leafy greens, herbs, edible flowers, and root vegetables grow in layered polyculture systems that mimic natural forest structure. Companion planting reduces pest pressure without chemicals. Compost from kitchen waste and garden trimmings feeds the soil. The remaining 40% of ingredients comes from local organic farmers within a 30-kilometer radius, supporting the regional food economy while minimizing food miles.

Water: The Living Pool

The natural swimming pool at Bambu Indah is a defining feature and a functioning ecosystem. Instead of chlorine or other chemicals, the pool uses biological filtration: a connected regeneration zone filled with aquatic plants filters and purifies the water continuously. Beneficial bacteria colonies break down organic matter, while gravel beds provide mechanical filtration. The result is swimming water that is purer than chemically treated alternatives and supports a small community of aquatic life. The system uses significantly less energy than conventional pool filtration, and the water is safe enough to irrigate gardens.

Waste: Closing the Loop

In Bali, where municipal waste management is notoriously inadequate, Bambu Indah has built its own comprehensive waste system. Organic waste is composted on-site and returned to the permaculture gardens. Recyclables are sorted and sent to verified recycling facilities. Single-use plastics have been eliminated from guest areas -- water is served in glass, toiletries are in refillable ceramic dispensers, and takeaway packaging uses banana leaves and bamboo containers. The property diverts an estimated 87% of its waste stream from landfill.

Community: Craft and Culture

Bambu Indah employs over 100 local staff, the majority from surrounding villages. But the community impact extends further: the property has become a training ground for bamboo construction, permaculture, and sustainable hospitality. The Hardys' Green School -- located nearby -- educates the next generation in sustainability principles. Local artisans supply furniture, textiles, and decorative elements. Traditional Balinese ceremonies and cultural events are held on the property, maintaining the spiritual and cultural connections that make Bali's relationship with sustainability unique.

Honest Assessment

Trade-offs and Limitations

Grid Dependence: Despite solar panel installation, Bambu Indah remains partially dependent on Bali's electrical grid, which is primarily powered by fossil fuels. Full energy independence has not yet been achieved.

Tourism Pressure on Ubud: The property exists within a region experiencing significant tourism-driven development pressure. While Bambu Indah itself practices sustainability, its success contributes to the desirability of the Ubud area, which faces water table depletion and land conversion challenges.

Flight Carbon Footprint: Bali is a long-haul flight destination for most travelers. The carbon emissions from international flights to reach the island significantly outweigh on-site sustainability gains for many guests.

Luxury Pricing: Rates place the property in the luxury category, raising accessibility questions similar to many high-end eco-resorts worldwide.
