Carbon Offsetting: An Honest Guide

Which carbon offsets actually work, which are sophisticated theater, and how to make honest climate decisions about your travel. No spin. Just evidence.

The Uncomfortable Truth About Carbon Offsets

Carbon offsetting is the most contentious topic in sustainable travel. It is simultaneously a legitimate tool for climate action and the most widespread form of climate greenwashing. The difference lies entirely in execution quality, and the industry makes it deliberately difficult to tell the two apart.

A 2023 Guardian and Die Zeit investigation found that more than 90% of rainforest offset credits from Verra, the world's largest carbon credit certifier, were likely phantom credits that did not represent genuine carbon reductions. The projects claimed to protect forests that were never under threat of deforestation, used inflated baseline deforestation rates, or suffered from leakage where deforestation simply moved to adjacent areas.

Yet high-quality offset projects exist. Gold Standard-certified cookstove projects in Africa have demonstrably reduced indoor air pollution, deforestation, and carbon emissions while improving health outcomes. Verified renewable energy projects in developing countries have displaced fossil fuel generation with measurable, monitored emissions reductions. The challenge is distinguishing these genuine projects from the vast ocean of questionable credits.

How Flight Emissions Are Calculated

Aviation accounts for approximately 2.5% of global CO2 emissions, but its true climate impact is 2-4 times higher due to radiative forcing: the additional warming effect of contrails, nitrogen oxides, and water vapor released at high altitude. Any honest carbon calculation must include this multiplier.

Emissions per passenger-kilometer vary by flight type:

  • Short-haul economy (under 1,500 km): approximately 0.255 kg CO2e per passenger-km. Higher due to fuel-intensive takeoff and landing comprising a larger proportion of total flight.
  • Medium-haul economy (1,500-4,000 km): approximately 0.225 kg CO2e per passenger-km.
  • Long-haul economy (over 4,000 km): approximately 0.195 kg CO2e per passenger-km. More efficient due to cruise phase dominating.
  • Business class: multiply economy figures by 2.5-3x due to increased seat space (fewer passengers per flight).
  • First class: multiply economy figures by 4-5x.

Example: A London to Nairobi round trip (13,500 km) in economy class emits approximately 2.6 tonnes CO2e including radiative forcing. That is equivalent to the entire annual carbon budget recommended by climate scientists (2.3 tonnes per person) to limit warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius.

Gold Standard vs Voluntary Markets

Not all offset certifications are created equal. Here is how the major standards compare:

Gold Standard (Highest Rigor)

Founded by WWF and other NGOs, Gold Standard requires projects to demonstrate additionality (the emission reduction would not have occurred without offset funding), undergo third-party verification by accredited auditors, deliver measurable co-benefits to local communities (health, employment, education), submit to ongoing monitoring and periodic re-verification, and publish transparent project documentation. Gold Standard credits cost $15-30 per tonne CO2e, reflecting the cost of genuine climate action.

Verra (Variable Quality)

Verra's Verified Carbon Standard (VCS) is the largest voluntary market standard by volume. Quality varies significantly by project type. Renewable energy and cookstove projects tend to be more reliable. Forest protection (REDD+) projects have been the subject of serious quality concerns following investigative reporting. Verra has announced methodology reforms, but these are ongoing.

Plan Vivo (Community-Focused)

Specializes in community-led projects in developing countries. Smaller scale but high integrity. Projects must demonstrate community ownership, land-use rights, and long-term benefit-sharing. Credits cost $15-25 per tonne.

Red Flags in Offset Markets

  • Credits priced under $5 per tonne (too cheap for genuine climate action)
  • Projects with no third-party verification
  • Forest protection projects with no satellite monitoring data
  • Offset providers that cannot name specific projects
  • "Carbon neutral" claims with no published emission reduction data

Reduction vs Offset: The Hierarchy

The honest climate hierarchy for travel decisions is:

  1. Avoid: Do you need to make this trip? Could a video call replace it? This is the most effective carbon reduction: 100% of emissions avoided.
  2. Reduce: Choose lower-emission transport. A train emits 80-90% less CO2e per passenger-km than a flight. Direct flights emit 20-30% less than connections. Economy emits 60-80% less than business class.
  3. Stay longer: Amortize your travel emissions over more days. A two-week trip emits the same flight carbon as a three-day trip but delivers four times more experience per tonne of CO2.
  4. Choose sustainably: Select accommodation powered by renewable energy, sourcing locally, and managing waste responsibly.
  5. Offset the remainder: After reducing as much as possible, offset the unavoidable remainder through Gold Standard or equivalent high-quality programs.

Offsetting without first reducing is like paying someone to diet for you while you eat more. The accounting might balance on paper, but the physics does not care about accounting.

How to Offset Responsibly

If you choose to offset, follow these principles:

  • Calculate honestly: Use atmosfair or myclimate calculators, which include radiative forcing. Airline calculators often undercount by 50% or more.
  • Choose Gold Standard or Plan Vivo: These standards have the strongest verification and community benefit requirements.
  • Prefer energy and cookstove projects: These deliver immediate, measurable emission reductions. Tree-planting projects take decades to sequester the claimed carbon and face permanence risks from fire, disease, and land-use change.
  • Pay the real price: If your offset costs less than $15 per tonne, the quality is questionable.
  • Do not stop at offsetting: Use offsetting as a complement to genuine emission reduction, never as a substitute.

The Future: Beyond Offsets

The offset market is evolving. Newer approaches include carbon removal credits (direct air capture, biochar, enhanced weathering) which physically remove CO2 from the atmosphere rather than avoiding future emissions. These are more expensive ($100-1,000+ per tonne) but more scientifically certain. As these technologies scale, they may eventually replace traditional offsets as the gold standard for unavoidable emissions.

For now, the best approach is transparent and layered: reduce what you can, offset the rest through verified programs, advocate for systemic change in aviation and tourism, and be honest about the limits of individual action in a system that requires structural transformation.
