Mangrove Restoration Programs: Rebuild Coastal Ecosystems in Colombia, Mexico, and Kenya

Stand knee-deep in tidal mud and plant the trees that will protect coastlines for generations. Mangrove restoration is one of the most effective climate interventions on Earth, and these programs put the work directly in your hands.

Photo by Unsplash photographer. Coastal ecosystems like these depend on healthy mangrove forests for protection and biodiversity.

The Experience

Mangroves are the unsung heroes of climate resilience.

"People plant trees in forests and feel good about it. But when you plant a mangrove, you are doing something far more powerful. You are building a wall against storm surges, a nursery for fish that feed millions, a carbon vault that outperforms any tropical forest, and a water filter that protects coral reefs downstream. One tree, four miracles."

— Professor Amara Osei, Marine Ecologist, University of Nairobi

The world has lost over 35% of its mangrove forests since 1980, primarily to shrimp farming, coastal development, and pollution. This destruction releases massive amounts of stored carbon and strips coastlines of their natural storm defense. Our restoration programs in Colombia, Mexico, and Kenya are reversing this trend, one seedling at a time, while training local communities in sustainable mangrove management that provides long-term economic alternatives to destruction.

Colombia

Cartagena Coast: Reviving the Caribbean's Mangrove Shield

The Setting

Colombia's Caribbean coast once boasted vast mangrove forests that protected cities like Cartagena from hurricanes and storm surges. Decades of urban expansion and shrimp farming destroyed much of this natural barrier. Our restoration program operates in the Cienaga de la Virgen and surrounding estuaries, working to rebuild what was lost while creating sustainable livelihoods for fishing communities displaced by mangrove destruction.

Nursery Operations

Volunteers help manage community-run mangrove nurseries that propagate four native species: red mangrove, black mangrove, white mangrove, and buttonwood. You learn to collect propagules from mature trees, prepare nursery beds, monitor germination rates, and harden seedlings for transplanting. Each nursery produces over 10,000 seedlings per season, with survival rates exceeding 85% thanks to careful site selection and planting techniques.

Planting and Monitoring

Planting sessions occur during low tide windows, when volunteers wade into designated restoration zones to install seedlings at precise spacing intervals. Post-planting monitoring tracks growth rates, survival percentages, and colonization by marine species. Within two years, restored mangrove plots typically show measurable increases in fish, crab, and bird populations.

Community Fisheries Integration

The program works closely with local fishing cooperatives, demonstrating that restored mangroves directly increase fish catches in adjacent waters. Former mangrove clearers now serve as restoration guides and nursery managers. This economic transformation is perhaps the program's most important achievement: proving that living mangroves are worth more than cleared land.

Mexico & Kenya

Expanding Restoration Across Continents

Sian Ka'an, Mexico

Mexico's Sian Ka'an Biosphere Reserve, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, contains some of the most pristine mangrove systems in the Caribbean. Our program here focuses on restoring areas damaged by Hurricane Dean and subsequent development pressure. Volunteers work alongside Maya communities who have managed these coastal resources for centuries, combining traditional knowledge with modern restoration science.

Mombasa Coast, Kenya

Kenya's Indian Ocean coast supports diverse mangrove forests that serve as critical nurseries for the region's commercial fisheries. Our Mombasa program partners with the Kenya Marine and Fisheries Research Institute to restore degraded mangrove zones while training local women's cooperatives in sustainable mangrove harvesting for traditional crafts and honey production.

Carbon Sequestration Education

All programs include workshops on blue carbon science, teaching volunteers how mangrove ecosystems capture and store atmospheric carbon in their biomass and sediments. You learn to measure carbon stocks using standard field protocols and understand how mangrove restoration fits into global climate mitigation strategies. This knowledge transforms volunteers into informed climate advocates.

Program Duration and Pricing

Programs range from 1-week introductions to 3-month intensive placements. All include accommodation in eco-lodges, locally sourced meals, training, equipment, and transportation. Longer programs offer opportunities to lead planting teams, manage nursery operations, and contribute to published research. Programs run year-round, with planting seasons varying by location.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Mangroves are among the most productive ecosystems on Earth. They sequester carbon at rates up to four times higher than terrestrial forests, earning the term "blue carbon." They protect coastlines from storm surges and erosion, filter pollutants from water, serve as nursery habitat for over 75% of commercially important fish species, and support extraordinary biodiversity. Despite covering less than 1% of tropical forest area, mangroves provide ecosystem services valued at over $33,000 per hectare annually.

Volunteers participate in multiple restoration activities: collecting propagules from healthy trees, nurturing seedlings in the nursery, planting seedlings in designated restoration zones at low tide, monitoring growth rates and survival of previously planted mangroves, removing invasive species, conducting biodiversity surveys, and participating in community education workshops. The work is physical and often involves wading in mud and water, but it is immensely rewarding.

Mangrove ecosystems store approximately 1,000 tonnes of carbon per hectare in their soil and biomass, making them one of the most carbon-dense ecosystems on the planet. A single hectare of restored mangrove can sequester 6-8 tonnes of CO2 equivalent per year once established. Our programs have restored over 200 hectares since 2018, representing an estimated carbon sequestration potential of over 1,400 tonnes of CO2 per year.

Swimming ability is not required for most mangrove restoration activities. Planting typically takes place in shallow tidal flats where water depth rarely exceeds knee height. However, some monitoring activities may involve boat-based surveys. If you are not a confident swimmer, let the program coordinators know and they will assign you to appropriate tasks. All participants are provided with life vests for any boat-based activities.

All three locations welcome beginners with comprehensive training. Colombia's Cartagena coast program is ideal for first-timers due to its well-established infrastructure, calm planting conditions, and bilingual staff. Mexico's Sian Ka'an program offers the most diverse mangrove species and combines restoration with eco-tourism. Kenya's Mombasa coast program is best for those seeking immersive cultural exchange alongside conservation work.
